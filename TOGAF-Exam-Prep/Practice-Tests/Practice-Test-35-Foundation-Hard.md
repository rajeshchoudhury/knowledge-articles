## Answer Key

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** c) Define the business architecture approach based on scope, constraints, and available resources, and select appropriate reference models, viewpoints, and tools  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** ADM Phase B Specific Steps  
**Explanation:** The first step in Phase B is to establish the approach — defining how the Business Architecture will be developed based on the scope set in Phase A, selecting appropriate reference models and viewpoints, and identifying tools. This "approach definition" step occurs before developing the target architecture (a) or performing gap analysis (d). Option (b) is partially correct but incomplete — selecting reference models is part of the broader approach definition step, not the entirety of it. TOGAF consistently starts each domain architecture phase with approach definition before content development.

---

### Question 2
**Correct Answer:** a) A Business Service is realized by one or more Logical Application Components, and a Logical Application Component may realize multiple Business Services  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Content Metamodel Entity Relationships  
**Explanation:** In the TOGAF Content Metamodel, the relationship between Business Services and Logical Application Components is many-to-many: a Business Service can be realized (automated) by one or more Logical Application Components, and a single Logical Application Component can realize multiple Business Services. This reflects the reality that application systems often support multiple business capabilities. Data Entities are managed by Application Components, not directly by Technology Components (b). Organization Units are business entities, not technology subtypes (c). Platform Services can be consumed by multiple Application Components (d).

---

### Question 3
**Correct Answer:** c) "Compliant" means the implementation is consistent with the architecture and satisfies the specification; "Conformant" means it not only satisfies the specification but also aligns with the broader architectural spirit, standards, and guidelines  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Compliance Level Edge Cases  
**Explanation:** This is one of the subtlest distinctions in TOGAF. Compliance means the implementation satisfies the specification — it does what was required. Conformance is a higher bar — it means the implementation not only meets the letter of the specification but also aligns with the broader intent, standards, and guidelines of the architecture. A solution can be compliant (meets requirements) but not fully conformant (uses non-standard approaches). Option (a) incorrectly treats them as synonyms. Option (b) reverses the relationship. Option (d) incorrectly limits them to specific domains.

---

### Question 4
**Correct Answer:** b) Re-enter at Phase A (Architecture Vision) scoped to the new business segment, leveraging the existing Strategic Architecture as context and constraint  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** ADM Re-entry Points  
**Explanation:** When the Strategic Architecture is sound but a new business segment is needed (e.g., due to acquisition), the appropriate re-entry point is Phase A, scoped to the new segment. This allows the architect to develop a Segment Architecture vision within the constraints of the existing Strategic Architecture. Restarting from Preliminary (a) is excessive since principles are already established. Jumping to Phase E (c) skips critical architecture development. Phase H (d) is for managing changes to existing architecture, not developing new segments. The ADM's support for different landscape partitions enables this targeted re-entry.

### Question 6
**Correct Answer:** c) Organization-Specific Architecture — the most specific end of the Enterprise Continuum  
**Difficulty:** Hard  
**Topic:** Enterprise Continuum Classification  
**Explanation:** The Enterprise Continuum classifies architectures from generic to specific: Foundation → Common Systems → Industry → Organization-Specific. An architecture that is specific to a single organization — even if derived from an industry reference model — is classified at the Organization-Specific end because it has been tailored for that organization's unique context. The origin (industry reference model) doesn't determine the classification; the specificity of the resulting architecture does. Foundation (a) is the most generic. Common Systems (b) crosses industries but isn't organization-specific. Industry (d) would apply to the reference model it was derived from, not the organization-specific result.

### Question 8
**Correct Answer:** b) A Logical Application Component represents a functional grouping that is implementation-independent; a Physical Application Component represents the actual application or module that realizes the logical component  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Content Metamodel Entity Relationships  
**Explanation:** The Logical/Physical distinction in the Content Metamodel is fundamental. Logical Application Components describe WHAT the application landscape should do (functional groupings, capabilities) without specifying HOW. Physical Application Components describe the actual applications, modules, or systems that implement those logical groupings. The mapping is typically many-to-many — one logical component might be realized by multiple physical applications, and one physical application might realize parts of multiple logical components. They are not identical (a), not one-to-one (c), and not phase-specific (d) — both are developed primarily during Phase C.

An external firm (c) would not have organizational authority. The non-compliant project manager (d) is a party to the dispute and cannot arbitrate. The escalation path must follow the organization's established governance hierarchy.

### Question 14
**Correct Answer:** b) ABBs define the required capability and shape at a technology-independent level; SBBs represent specific implementations or products that realize those ABBs  
**Difficulty:** Hard  
**Topic:** ABB vs. SBB  
**Explanation:** Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs) capture architecture requirements — they define what capabilities are needed and the functional specification, without prescribing implementation. Solution Building Blocks (SBBs) represent actual implementations — specific products, technologies, or solutions that fulfill the ABB specifications. ABBs live in the Architecture Continuum; SBBs live in the Solutions Continuum. They are not identical (a). ABBs come first from architecture work, not SBBs from requirements (c). Both exist within the Enterprise Continuum framework (d), not in separate unlinked structures.

### Question 15
**Correct Answer:** b) Requirements Management operates continuously throughout the entire ADM cycle, providing a dynamic process for identifying, storing, feeding, and managing architecture requirements across all phases  
**Difficulty:** Hard  
**Topic:** Requirements Management Operation  
**Explanation:** Requirements Management is depicted at the center of the ADM cycle diagram for a reason — it operates continuously and connects to every phase. It receives requirements from and feeds requirements to each phase. It ensures that requirements are captured regardless of when they emerge, stored consistently, and made available when needed. It does not run after Phase H (a). It is not limited to Phase A (c). It is a core, required process, not optional (d). Its continuous nature is a fundamental characteristic distinguishing it from the sequential numbered phases.

### Question 16
**Correct Answer:** c) Consistent — the implementation is compliant with some architecture specifications but does not fully conform to all standards  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Compliance Level Edge Cases  
**Explanation:** TOGAF defines several compliance levels. "Consistent" describes a situation where the implementation is partially compliant — it aligns with some specifications but deviates from others (such as using a non-standard technology component). This is different from "Compliant" (meeting all specifications) or "Non-compliant" (failing to meet specifications). The deviation should be documented with justification and ideally a time-bound remediation plan to achieve full compliance.
