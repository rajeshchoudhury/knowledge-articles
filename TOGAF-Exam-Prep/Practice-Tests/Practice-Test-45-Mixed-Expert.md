## Scenario 1: The Maturity Leap

**DigitalGov** is a national government agency responsible for digitizing citizen services. An external assessment has rated the agency's enterprise architecture maturity at Level 2 (Under Development): the agency has an architecture practice with basic processes, some governance exists but is inconsistent, and architecture artifacts are created but not systematically maintained.

The new Agency Director has set an ambitious goal: achieve Architecture Maturity Level 4 (Managed) within 12 months. Level 4 requires: architecture standards are consistently applied across the organization, architecture governance is operational and enforced, the Architecture Repository is actively maintained and used, architecture metrics are tracked and reported, and architecture informs all significant IT investment decisions.

Simultaneously, the agency is executing a $200M Digital Transformation Program that will replace 15 legacy citizen service systems with a unified digital platform. The program is in its early stages—the Architecture Vision was just approved. The program is politically sensitive, with congressional oversight and a hard 18-month delivery deadline.

The Chief Architect must balance building architecture capability (maturity improvement) with delivering architecture for the digital transformation program. Resources are limited: the architecture team has 8 architects and a $2M annual budget for the EA practice.

### Question 21
What is the BEST approach to navigate the dual priorities of maturity improvement and digital transformation?

a) Focus exclusively on the digital transformation program and defer the maturity improvement. The $200M program is politically sensitive and has a hard deadline. Maturity improvement can wait until the program is delivered. Attempting both simultaneously will result in both failing.

b) Use the digital transformation program AS the vehicle for maturity improvement. Instead of treating maturity improvement and program delivery as competing priorities, integrate them: build governance processes through the program (establish Architecture Board reviews, compliance checkpoints, and dispensation processes using real program decisions), populate the Architecture Repository through program artifacts (creating proper templates, classification, and governance as artifacts are produced), develop architecture metrics using program data (cost savings, rework reduction, delivery acceleration), and demonstrate architecture's value to IT investment decisions through program governance. This approach delivers maturity improvement as a by-product of doing architecture well on the highest-visibility initiative.

c) Split the team: assign 4 architects to the digital transformation program and 4 architects to maturity improvement activities. Run both efforts in parallel with separate governance and reporting.

d) Hire a consulting firm to handle the digital transformation architecture while the internal team focuses on maturity improvement. This separates the two concerns and ensures both get adequate attention.

### Question 22
How should the Chief Architect manage the risk that the maturity improvement goal may be unrealistic (Level 2 to Level 4 in 12 months)?

a) Ignore the risk and commit to the Director's goal. The Chief Architect should not push back on executive direction.

b) Present the Director with an honest maturity roadmap: acknowledge that while Level 4 in 12 months is ambitious, a realistic target might be achieving Level 3 (Defined) within 12 months with a clear path to Level 4 within 24 months. Support this with: specific milestones for each maturity attribute (governance, repository, standards, metrics), resource requirements for each milestone, dependencies on organizational change (not just EA team activities), risk factors that could delay maturity progress (staff turnover, budget constraints, organizational resistance). Propose interim maturity checkpoints at 3, 6, and 9 months to demonstrate progress and adjust the plan based on actual advancement. Frame Level 3 achievement as a significant accomplishment that demonstrates tangible governance improvement and positions the agency for Level 4.

c) Accept the goal publicly but privately plan for Level 3 achievement. If the team reaches Level 3, the Director may be satisfied with the progress and not insist on Level 4.

d) Refuse the assignment and recommend that the Director hire a Chief Architect who has achieved Level 4 maturity at another organization. A Level 2 team cannot self-improve to Level 4 without external leadership.

## Scenario 2: The Implementation Deviation Tribunal

**AutoCorp** is a large automobile manufacturer executing a digital factory program governed by a TOGAF-based architecture. The Target Architecture specifies a unified IoT platform, standardized data models, and a common application integration layer for all 12 manufacturing plants.

During Phase G: Implementation Governance, the Architecture Board discovers that three implementation projects have deviated from the Target Architecture:

**Project 1: The Performance Deviation** — Plant A's implementation team replaced the specified integration middleware with a high-performance message broker because the standard middleware couldn't handle the plant's real-time sensor data volume (50,000 events/second vs. the architecture's assumed 10,000 events/second). The deviation was not approved in advance but has resulted in 5x better performance.

**Project 2: The Business-Driven Deviation** — Plant B's team added an unauthorized custom analytics module directly connected to the production database. The plant manager demanded it after discovering that the planned analytics architecture wouldn't be delivered for 12 months. The module provides real-time production quality insights that have already reduced defect rates by 15%.

**Project 3: The Vendor-Driven Deviation** — Plant C's team adopted a vendor's proprietary IoT gateway instead of the architecture-standard open protocol gateway. The vendor offered a 70% discount conditional on using their proprietary solution, saving $2M. The plant's IT director approved the decision without consulting the architecture team.

The Architecture Board must decide the appropriate governance response for each deviation.

### Question 23
What governance decision should the Architecture Board make for each of the three deviations?

a) Enforce strict compliance for all three projects. Regardless of the business justification, allowing deviations undermines the authority of the enterprise architecture. Require all three plants to revert to the specified architecture within 6 months and issue formal non-compliance notices.

b) Apply differentiated governance responses based on the nature of each deviation. **Project 1 (Performance):** The deviation revealed a genuine architecture deficiency—the Target Architecture underestimated data volumes. Grant a formal dispensation, initiate a review of the Target Architecture's performance assumptions across all 12 plants, and consider updating the Target Architecture to adopt the high-performance message broker as the new standard if the performance requirement is widespread. **Project 2 (Business-Driven):** Acknowledge the business value but address the governance bypass. Grant a time-bound dispensation for the custom analytics module, but require the team to develop a migration plan to the planned analytics architecture when it's available. Add the real-time analytics requirement to the Architecture Requirements Specification to ensure the planned analytics architecture addresses this need. **Project 3 (Vendor-Driven):** This is the most concerning deviation—it was driven by cost rather than capability and creates vendor lock-in with proprietary technology. Conduct a thorough risk assessment of the vendor lock-in. If the risk is manageable, grant a conditional dispensation with a requirement to maintain the ability to migrate to open protocols. If the risk is significant, require a phased transition to the standard gateway. Across all three cases, strengthen Phase G governance processes to catch deviations earlier.

c) Accept all three deviations as pragmatic decisions and update the enterprise architecture to reflect reality. Each deviation produced positive results (performance, quality improvement, cost savings), and the architecture should adapt to what works rather than enforcing theoretical purity.

d) Convene an emergency Architecture Board session and revoke all three projects' Architecture Contracts. The projects have violated their governance agreements, and the Architecture Board must demonstrate zero tolerance for non-compliance to prevent other plants from deviating.

### Question 24
What systemic governance improvement should the Architecture Board implement to prevent similar deviations in the remaining 9 plants?

a) Assign a full-time architect to each of the 12 plants to monitor implementation and prevent deviations before they occur.

b) Strengthen the Phase G implementation governance framework by: establishing clear escalation procedures for implementation teams when they encounter architecture constraints that impede delivery, defining criteria for when implementation teams can request emergency architecture decisions (rather than deviating without approval), implementing regular architecture compliance check-ins during implementation (not just final reviews), creating a feedback mechanism where implementation teams can formally challenge architecture decisions with evidence (performance data, cost analysis), updating the Architecture Contract template to explicitly address deviation scenarios and the required governance process, and reviewing whether the Target Architecture's assumptions (like the 10,000 events/second) need to be validated more rigorously in future architecture development phases.

c) Reduce the scope of architecture governance to high-level principles only and let implementation teams make all technology decisions within those principles. Detailed architecture specifications clearly don't work in practice.

d) Implement automated architecture compliance monitoring that detects deviations in real-time and automatically escalates to the Architecture Board. Technology-based enforcement is more reliable than process-based governance.

## Scenario 3: The Architecture Repository Disaster Recovery

**CloudFirst Bank** is a digital-only bank that has been operating for 7 years with a mature enterprise architecture practice. A catastrophic cloud infrastructure failure—caused by the bank's cloud provider experiencing a multi-region outage combined with a backup corruption event—has resulted in the loss of the bank's entire Architecture Repository.

The surviving architecture artifacts are limited to:
- Phase B Business Architecture diagrams (printed copies found in the Chief Architect's office)
- The most recent Architecture Roadmap (a PDF copy was attached to an email sent to the CEO 3 months ago)
- The last Architecture Compliance Review report (from 6 months ago, found on a local laptop)
- Architecture Principles document (published on the company intranet, which was hosted separately from the cloud infrastructure)

Lost artifacts include: the Architecture Definition Document, all Application Architecture and Technology Architecture documentation, the Standards Information Base, all Catalogs and Matrices, the Content Metamodel definition, the Tailored Architecture Framework documentation, the Governance Log, and all historical architecture versions.

The bank has 200+ applications in production, serves 3 million customers, and has a digital transformation program mid-execution (currently in Phase E).

### Question 25
What is the BEST recovery plan for the Architecture Repository?

a) Halt all architecture activities, including the in-flight digital transformation program, until the Architecture Repository is fully rebuilt. Architecture work without a repository is ungoverned and risks creating more inconsistency.

b) Implement a prioritized, multi-track recovery plan. **Immediate (Week 1-2):** Establish a new Architecture Repository infrastructure with proper backup and disaster recovery (the ironic lesson). Ingest the surviving artifacts as the foundation—Architecture Principles, Business Architecture diagrams, Architecture Roadmap, and Compliance Review report. **Short-term (Month 1-2):** Reverse-engineer current-state architecture documentation from production systems—use application inventories, network diagrams, database schemas, and deployment configurations to rebuild the Application Architecture and Technology Architecture catalogs. Reconstruct the Standards Information Base by gathering current technology standards from procurement records, vendor contracts, and IT governance documents. **Medium-term (Month 2-4):** Rebuild the Tailored Architecture Framework and Content Metamodel through workshops with the architecture team (who carry this knowledge even if the documentation is lost). Reconstruct the Governance Log from meeting minutes, email records, and the surviving Compliance Review. **Ongoing:** For the in-flight digital transformation program, continue Phase E using the surviving Roadmap as the baseline and reconstruct the Architecture Definition Document by assembling outputs from the Phase B-D work that the team members still have in draft form or memory. Implement mandatory architecture artifact backup procedures as a new governance requirement.

c) Abandon the current Architecture Repository approach and start fresh with a completely new architecture baseline. Use the disaster as an opportunity to re-assess the enterprise architecture from scratch without being constrained by historical decisions.

d) Commission the cloud provider to recover the data. Infrastructure providers have disaster recovery obligations, and the bank should not accept the loss until all recovery options are exhausted. In the meantime, proceed with architecture work based on team members' knowledge.

### Question 26
How should the Architecture Board govern the in-flight digital transformation program (currently in Phase E) given the Repository loss?

a) Restart the digital transformation program from Phase A. Without the Architecture Repository, the program lacks the governance foundation to continue.

b) Continue the program with adapted governance. The Architecture Board should: assess what Phase E artifacts the team members have in draft or working form (locally saved documents, shared drives, email attachments), reconstruct the minimum viable Architecture Definition Document needed for Phase E decisions by assembling available Phase B-D outputs, use the surviving Architecture Roadmap as the anchor document for Phase E work, establish an interim governance process that requires more frequent Architecture Board reviews (compensating for reduced documentation with increased oversight), mandate that all new architecture artifacts created during the recovery period are immediately stored in the new Repository with proper classification, and accept that some architecture decisions may need to be validated retroactively once the Repository is rebuilt—document assumptions and validation requirements explicitly.

c) Suspend the program for 3 months while the Repository is rebuilt, then resume Phase E with full documentation. The 3-month delay is acceptable given the circumstances.

d) Convert the digital transformation program to an agile delivery model without formal architecture governance. The Repository loss makes formal governance impractical, and agile practices can provide sufficient oversight.

## Scenario 4: The Monolithic vs. Modular Documentation Decision

**NewPath Insurance** is a mid-size insurance company that has never practiced enterprise architecture. The company has just hired its first enterprise architect and chartered a new EA practice. The CEO wants the enterprise architect to develop a comprehensive enterprise architecture covering all four domains (Business, Data, Application, Technology) to support a strategic modernization initiative.

The enterprise architect faces a fundamental documentation decision: should she create a single, monolithic Architecture Definition Document (ADD) that covers all four domains in one document, or create four separate domain-specific Architecture Definition Documents?

Arguments for monolithic ADD:
- Single source of truth for the entire architecture
- Easier to see cross-domain dependencies
- Simpler document governance (one review process, one approval)
- The organization is small (800 employees, ~50 applications) and a single document is manageable
- The CEO prefers a "one architecture" narrative for board presentations

Arguments for modular domain-specific documents:
- Each domain can be developed and reviewed by domain experts independently
- Allows phased delivery (Business Architecture first, then Data, Application, Technology)
- Different stakeholders care about different domains
- Easier to update individual domains without full document review
- Follows the natural flow of ADM phases (B, C, D)

The organization has no Architecture Repository, no architecture principles, no Architecture Board, and no Tailored Architecture Framework. The enterprise architect is starting from zero.

### Question 27
What documentation approach should the enterprise architect recommend?

a) Create a single monolithic Architecture Definition Document. For an organization new to enterprise architecture, simplicity is paramount. A single document ensures everyone is looking at the same architecture, prevents version conflicts between domain documents, and satisfies the CEO's desire for a unified narrative. The document can have domain-specific sections while maintaining a single governing structure.

b) Create a modular documentation approach with a parent Architecture Definition Document and domain-specific supplements. The parent ADD provides the cross-domain overview, executive summary, architecture principles, stakeholder concerns, and cross-domain dependency analysis that the CEO needs. Domain-specific supplements (Business Architecture, Data Architecture, Application Architecture, Technology Architecture) contain the detailed domain content that experts need. This approach delivers the benefits of both approaches: unified narrative for leadership, domain depth for practitioners. Additionally, recommend establishing the foundational architecture governance elements first—even before detailed architecture work begins: define architecture principles, establish a lightweight Architecture Board (even 3-4 members), create a basic Architecture Repository structure, and adopt a simplified Tailored Architecture Framework. These foundations enable effective governance of whatever documentation approach is chosen.

c) Create four separate domain-specific Architecture Definition Documents with strict cross-referencing. This follows the natural ADM phase structure and allows the enterprise architect to deliver value incrementally—starting with the Business Architecture in Phase B.

d) Don't create an Architecture Definition Document at all initially. Focus first on establishing the architecture capability (governance, principles, repository, board) before producing any architecture artifacts. An ADD without governance is just a document, not an architecture.

### Question 28
Given that the organization has never practiced EA, what is the MOST CRITICAL governance element the enterprise architect should establish first?

a) An Architecture Repository to store all architecture artifacts. Without a repository, artifacts will be scattered and lost.

b) Architecture Principles that reflect the organization's values and strategic direction. Principles provide the decision-making foundation for all subsequent architecture work—they guide Phase B through D development, inform governance decisions, and align the architecture with business strategy. Without principles, architecture decisions lack a consistent rationale, and the Architecture Board (once established) has no basis for evaluating compliance. Principles also help communicate the architecture function's purpose to an organization unfamiliar with EA—they translate technical governance into business-relevant terms the CEO and executives can understand and support.

c) An Architecture Board to provide governance oversight. Without a Board, there's no authority to approve or enforce architecture decisions.

d) A Tailored Architecture Framework adapted to the organization's specific needs. Without a tailored framework, the enterprise architect won't know which TOGAF elements to apply.

## Answer Key - Part 1

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** c) Logical Application Component  
**Topic:** Content Metamodel Extensions  
**Explanation:** The TOGAF Content Metamodel distinguishes between core entities and extension entities. Logical Application Component is an EXTENSION entity that adds the logical/physical distinction to the core entity "Application Component." Core entities include Organization Unit, Actor, Role, Business Service, Information System Service, Data Entity, Application Component, and Technology Component. Business Service (option a), Data Entity (option b), and Technology Component (option d) are all core entities. The logical/physical distinction is part of the metamodel extension that adds granularity for organizations that need it.

### Question 2
**Correct Answer:** b) The project team escalates to the executive sponsor, who may direct the Architecture Board to reconsider or may override the decision within their authority  
**Topic:** Governance Escalation Paths  
**Explanation:** In TOGAF Architecture Governance, the escalation path for disputed decisions follows the organizational hierarchy. When the Architecture Board denies a dispensation request, the project team's recourse is to escalate to the executive sponsor. The executive sponsor has organizational authority that may exceed the Architecture Board's scope and can direct reconsideration or make an executive decision. The Chief Architect cannot override the Board (option a)—the Board is the governance authority. Architecture Board decisions are not final without recourse (option c)—TOGAF recognizes organizational authority hierarchies. Submitting to a different sub-committee (option d) would be governance shopping, not legitimate escalation.

### Question 3
**Correct Answer:** b) Select reference models, viewpoints, and tools  
**Topic:** Phase B Step Sequence  
**Explanation:** According to the TOGAF Standard, the first step in Phase B development is to select reference models, viewpoints, and tools that will be used to develop the Business Architecture. This ensures that the team has the right frameworks and instruments before beginning the actual architecture work. Developing the Baseline description (option a) comes after the reference models and viewpoints are selected. Identifying candidate components (option c) comes later in the process. Reviewing principles (option d) is part of the initial setup but the specific "select reference models, viewpoints, and tools" step is the documented first step of the development approach.

### Question 4
**Correct Answer:** c) Strategic Architecture  
**Topic:** Architecture Landscape Partitioning  
**Explanation:** Strategic Architecture provides the longest planning horizon because it addresses the enterprise-wide, long-term architectural direction. It typically covers 3-10 years and provides the context within which Segment Architectures and Capability Architectures operate. Capability Architecture (option a) has the shortest planning horizon, typically addressing specific capability implementations. Segment Architecture (option b) has a medium planning horizon. The levels do NOT share the same horizon (option d)—the differentiated planning horizons are a fundamental characteristic of Architecture Landscape partitioning.

### Question 5
**Correct Answer:** c) Vendor viability  
**Topic:** Implementation Factor Categories  
**Explanation:** The TOGAF Standard identifies specific implementation factor categories for Phase E consideration, including risks, cultural factors, and business value among others. "Vendor viability" is NOT specifically listed as an implementation factor category in the TOGAF Standard. While vendor considerations may be relevant to implementation decisions, they are not a formally named implementation factor category. Risks (option a), cultural factors (option b), and business value (option d) are all recognized categories. This question tests whether candidates know the exact TOGAF categories versus reasonable-sounding additions.

### Question 6
**Correct Answer:** c) Vision, Desire, Need, and Readiness  
**Topic:** Business Transformation Readiness Assessment  
**Explanation:** The TOGAF Business Transformation Readiness Assessment classifies readiness factors using the dimensions of Vision (does the organization have a clear vision for the change?), Desire (does the organization want the change?), Need (is there an acknowledged business need?), and Readiness (is the organization prepared to execute?). These four dimensions provide a comprehensive assessment of organizational preparedness. Probability and Impact (option a) are risk assessment dimensions. Option (b) uses terminology not from TOGAF's readiness assessment. Urgency, Maturity, and Complexity (option d) are also not the TOGAF readiness factor dimensions.

### Question 7
**Correct Answer:** d) Risk Initial Level and Residual Level Assessment  
**Topic:** Risk Management Technique  
**Explanation:** The TOGAF Standard's specific risk classification technique involves assessing risks at two levels: the Initial Level of Risk (before mitigation) and the Residual Level of Risk (after mitigation actions are identified). While a Risk Impact/Probability Matrix (option a) is a general risk management technique used in many frameworks, the TOGAF Standard specifically describes the Initial/Residual level approach. SWOT Analysis (option b) and Monte Carlo Simulation (option c) are not TOGAF-specific risk classification techniques, though they may be used in broader enterprise analysis.

### Question 8
**Correct Answer:** c) Reusable Architecture Building Block pattern  
**Topic:** Architecture Patterns  
**Explanation:** The TOGAF Standard recognizes architecture patterns in the context of reusable architecture building blocks. Patterns in TOGAF represent reusable solutions that can be captured as part of the Architecture Repository and Enterprise Continuum. The Singleton Pattern (option a) is a software design pattern from GoF, not a TOGAF architecture pattern type. Integration Pattern (option b), while relevant to architecture, is not a specifically categorized TOGAF pattern type. Option (d) is incorrect because TOGAF does discuss patterns in the context of building blocks and the Architecture Repository.

### Question 9
**Correct Answer:** b) Operate within the context established by a Strategic Architecture  
**Topic:** Architecture Landscape Partitioning Rules  
**Explanation:** A fundamental partitioning rule is that Segment Architectures must operate within the context established by the Strategic Architecture. The Strategic Architecture sets the enterprise-wide boundaries, principles, and direction within which segment-level work occurs. A Segment Architecture does NOT need to cover exactly one business unit (option a)—it can span organizational boundaries. Capability Architectures can begin before all Segment Architectures are complete (option c). Segment Architectures may emphasize certain domains more than others based on their scope (option d).

### Question 10
**Correct Answer:** c) Detailed technology standards for architecture tools  
**Topic:** Organizational Model for EA  
**Explanation:** The Organizational Model for Enterprise Architecture established in the Preliminary Phase includes scope of organizations impacted, maturity assessment, governance and support strategy, relationships to other frameworks, and the budget and resource plan. Detailed technology standards for architecture TOOLS are not a component of the Organizational Model—they are operational decisions made by the architecture team. The Organizational Model focuses on the organizational structure, roles, and governance of the architecture capability, not on the specific tools used by architects. Options (a), (b), and (d) are all legitimate components.

### Question 11
**Correct Answer:** c) Implementation Factor Assessment and Deduction Matrix  
**Topic:** Phase F Techniques  
**Explanation:** The Implementation Factor Assessment and Deduction Matrix is the specific TOGAF technique recommended for Phase F: Migration Planning to evaluate and prioritize migration projects. This matrix assesses projects against multiple implementation factors and deduces the optimal sequencing. Cost-benefit analysis (option a) is a general project management technique. Business Value Assessment (option b) is related but not the specific TOGAF technique. Architecture Impact Analysis (option d) is not the named TOGAF technique for Phase F prioritization.

### Question 12
**Correct Answer:** b) The Architecture Continuum classifies architecture descriptions, while the Solutions Continuum classifies solution implementations that realize those architectures  
**Topic:** Enterprise Continuum Components  
**Explanation:** The Enterprise Continuum comprises two complementary continuums: the Architecture Continuum and the Solutions Continuum. The Architecture Continuum classifies architecture descriptions (ABBs, reference architectures, etc.), while the Solutions Continuum classifies the actual implementations (SBBs, products, systems) that realize those architectures. They are distinct but parallel classification systems, not the same continuum (option a). They don't map to specific ADM phases (option c). The Solutions Continuum is parallel to, not a subset of, the Architecture Continuum (option d).

### Question 13
**Correct Answer:** c) Application Communication Diagram → Diagram  
**Topic:** Artifact Types  
**Explanation:** The Application Communication Diagram is correctly classified as a Diagram in the TOGAF Content Framework. It visually shows the relationships and communication paths between applications. The Organization/Actor Catalog (option a) is a Catalog, not a Matrix. The Business Interaction Matrix (option b) is a Matrix, not a Diagram. The Role/Actor Matrix (option d) is, despite the confusing name, not universally classified as a Catalog—it maps relationships between Roles and Actors. The Application Communication Diagram is unambiguously a Diagram.

### Question 14
**Correct Answer:** c) Phase E: Opportunities and Solutions  
**Topic:** Phase E Artifacts  
**Explanation:** The Consolidated Gaps, Solutions, and Dependencies Matrix is first created during Phase E: Opportunities and Solutions. This is where the gap analyses from Phases B, C, and D are consolidated into a single view that identifies the solutions needed and the dependencies between them. It is not created during Phase C or D (options a, b)—those phases create domain-specific gap analyses. Phase F (option d) uses the consolidated matrix as an input for migration planning but does not create it. Phase E's consolidation role is one of its defining characteristics.

### Question 15
**Correct Answer:** b) The Architecture Board ensures that the discipline of architecture-based development is adopted across the organization  
**Topic:** Architecture Board Responsibilities  
**Explanation:** Among the Architecture Board's governance responsibilities, ensuring the adoption of architecture-based development discipline across the organization relates to the Architecture Repository because the Repository is the mechanism through which architecture assets are shared and reused. The Board does NOT maintain all artifacts themselves (option a)—that's the architecture team's role. The Board is not responsible for technical Repository design (option c) or access controls (option d). The Board's role is strategic governance, ensuring that the architecture discipline (including proper Repository use) is adopted organization-wide.

### Question 16
**Correct Answer:** c) Any architecture entity across all four domains  
**Topic:** Content Metamodel - Constraints  
**Explanation:** In the TOGAF Content Metamodel, Constraints can be associated with entities across all four architecture domains. Constraints represent restrictions or limitations that affect how the architecture can be implemented, and they can apply to business processes, data entities, application components, and technology platforms. They are not limited to Business Architecture (option a) or Technology Architecture (option b) alone. While the Architecture Requirements Specification documents constraints (option d), the metamodel allows constraints to be associated directly with entities in any domain.

### Question 17
**Correct Answer:** b) To identify the major architecture concerns and risks that need to be addressed by the architecture engagement  
**Topic:** Architecture Vision Content  
**Explanation:** The "Key Issues" section of the Architecture Vision document identifies the major architecture concerns and risks that the architecture engagement must address. These are the significant challenges, gaps, or risks that justify the architecture effort and guide its focus. It is not a bug list (option a), a stakeholder complaints register (option c), or a project management issues log (option d). The Key Issues section frames the architectural problem space and ensures the engagement addresses the most critical concerns.

### Question 18
**Correct Answer:** b) The practice of dividing the architectural space into manageable, independently developed segments that can be governed and managed effectively  
**Topic:** Architecture Partitioning  
**Explanation:** Architecture Partitioning in TOGAF refers to the practice of dividing the architecture into manageable parts that can be developed, governed, and managed independently while maintaining overall coherence. This is broader than just domain division (option a)—it includes concepts like Strategic, Segment, and Capability architectures. It is not about separating baseline from target (option c) or splitting the ADM into parallel tracks (option d). Partitioning is an organizational and governance concept that enables enterprise architecture at scale.

### Question 19
**Correct Answer:** c) Architecture Requirements Specification  
**Topic:** Interoperability Requirements  
**Explanation:** Interoperability requirements identified across the architecture are specifically managed and tracked in the Architecture Requirements Specification. This document provides the quantitative statements that implementation projects must satisfy, including interoperability requirements that span multiple systems and domains. The Architecture Vision (option a) may identify interoperability as a goal but doesn't track specific requirements. The Architecture Definition Document (option b) describes the architecture but the specific requirements are in the Requirements Specification. The Implementation and Migration Plan (option d) addresses how to implement but doesn't serve as the tracking mechanism.

### Question 20
**Correct Answer:** c) The choice of programming languages for implementation  
**Topic:** ADM Adaptation  
**Explanation:** The TOGAF Standard discusses adapting the ADM for different architecture styles and considerations, including SOA considerations, formality of documentation, and modeling notations. However, the choice of programming languages for implementation is NOT an ADM adaptation consideration—it's an implementation decision that falls below the level of enterprise architecture. Programming language choices are SBB-level decisions made during Phase G implementation, not ADM adaptation parameters. SOA considerations (option a), documentation formality (option b), and modeling notations (option d) all influence how the ADM is applied.
