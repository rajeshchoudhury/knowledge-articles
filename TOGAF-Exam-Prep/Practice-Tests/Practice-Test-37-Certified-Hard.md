## Scenario 1: Implementing Full TOGAF at Architecture Maturity Level 1

Pinnacle Manufacturing is a $5 billion industrial conglomerate that produces heavy machinery across 12 factories worldwide. The company's IT organization has historically operated in a purely reactive mode — systems are built or acquired to solve immediate problems with no enterprise-level coordination. A recent ERP implementation failure ($45 million lost) has finally convinced the board of directors that enterprise architecture is needed. They have hired a new VP of Enterprise Architecture, Thomas Reeves, and given him a team of 4 architects with a mandate to "implement TOGAF enterprise architecture."

However, an honest assessment reveals the organization is at Architecture Maturity Level 1 (Initial): there are no architecture processes, no governance structures, no architecture principles, no repository, and no stakeholder awareness of what enterprise architecture even means. The IT organization of 800 people has never worked with architects before and views them with suspicion. Business stakeholders see IT as a cost center. The CEO, emboldened by a conference presentation she attended, has told the board that "we will have a fully functioning TOGAF ADM operating within 12 months." She has publicly committed to this timeline in an investor call.

Thomas knows this timeline is unrealistic for full ADM implementation. He has a $2 million annual budget (modest for a $5 billion company) and faces resistance from the CIO, who sees the EA team as a threat to her authority. The IT Project Management Office (PMO) has its own governance processes and doesn't want additional architecture oversight. Three major projects are already in flight without any architecture involvement, and two more are about to start. Thomas needs a strategy that builds EA capability realistically while showing visible progress to satisfy the CEO's public commitment.

### Question 1
Based on the scenario above, what is the best approach for Thomas to adopt?

a) Execute the full TOGAF Preliminary Phase within the first 3 months, establishing architecture principles, governance framework, Architecture Board, and Architecture Repository. Then launch a formal Phase A Architecture Vision for the most strategically important of the five in-flight/starting projects. Target having one complete ADM cycle done within 12 months. Report to the CEO that the ADM is "operational" even though it's only been applied to one initiative.

b) Focus the first 6 months on building architecture capability rather than running the ADM. Develop an architecture skills roadmap, create lightweight architecture principles collaboratively with the CIO and PMO, establish an informal architecture review process for the five current projects (not full governance), and build relationships with business stakeholders. Begin a formal ADM cycle in month 7, targeting a well-scoped Segment Architecture. Present to the CEO a maturity progression plan rather than a single "ADM operational" milestone.

c) Adopt an "architecture-lite" approach: create a simplified version of the ADM with only Preliminary, Phase A, and Phase F, skipping detailed Business and Technology Architecture phases initially. Apply this lightweight ADM to all five current projects simultaneously to demonstrate immediate breadth of coverage. Use the simplified approach to build organizational familiarity with architecture concepts before introducing the full ADM. Report to the CEO that the ADM is operational across five projects.

d) Negotiate directly with the CEO to reset expectations: present the Architecture Maturity Model showing the organization is at Level 1 and explain that reaching Level 3 (Defined) typically takes 2-3 years. Propose a realistic 3-year roadmap with annual milestones. In the interim, embed an architect in each of the five projects to provide architectural guidance without formal governance. Prioritize the Architecture Repository and Standards Information Base as first deliverables.

## Scenario 2: Conflicting Stakeholder Goals in the Architecture Vision

NexGen Financial Services is a mid-tier investment firm managing $120 billion in assets. The firm has launched an enterprise architecture initiative to modernize its technology platform and improve operational efficiency. The enterprise architect, Maria Santos, is conducting Phase A (Architecture Vision) for a major transformation program. Her stakeholder analysis has revealed a deep conflict between two of the most powerful stakeholders.

The CFO, James Wu, is laser-focused on cost reduction. The firm's cost-to-income ratio is 78%, well above the industry average of 65%. James wants the architecture to drive a 20% reduction in technology spending ($60 million annually) through system consolidation, vendor rationalization, and elimination of redundant platforms. He has presented a list of 40 systems he wants decommissioned within 18 months. James controls budget approval for all technology initiatives.

The CTO, Priya Sharma, believes the firm's survival depends on innovation investment. Competitors are deploying AI-driven trading algorithms, blockchain-based settlement, and real-time risk analytics. Priya wants the architecture to support $80 million in new technology investment over the next 2 years, including a new cloud-native trading platform, an AI/ML data science environment, and a modern API gateway for fintech partnerships. She has the CEO's ear and board support for innovation.

Both stakeholders are on the Architecture Board. They have publicly disagreed in board meetings, and their teams have begun lobbying individual architects. The Architecture Vision document is due in 3 weeks. Maria has attempted to schedule joint workshops, but James and Priya refuse to be in the same room. The CEO has told Maria privately that "both are right — find a way to make it work." The firm's existing architecture consists of 180 applications, many of which are redundant or underutilized.

### Question 2
Based on the scenario above, how should Maria craft the Architecture Vision?

a) Develop the Architecture Vision around a "Rationalize to Innovate" narrative that sequences cost reduction before innovation. Use the Architecture Vision to show that consolidating the 180 applications to approximately 100 (decommissioning the 40 redundant systems plus identifying 40 more) will free up $45 million annually that can be redirected to fund innovation. Create a phased roadmap: Year 1 for rationalization, Year 2 for innovation investment. Present this as a unified business case where both objectives are achieved but sequenced. Engage both stakeholders separately to validate their priorities are addressed before presenting to the Architecture Board.

b) Present two alternative Architecture Visions to the Architecture Board and let the board decide. Vision A aligns with the CFO's cost reduction focus; Vision B aligns with the CTO's innovation focus. Include a cost-benefit analysis for each. The Architecture Board's role is to make strategic architectural decisions, and this is fundamentally a business strategy question that the architect shouldn't unilaterally resolve. Document the decision rationale regardless of which vision is selected.

c) Create an Architecture Vision that addresses both objectives simultaneously without sequencing. Identify "dual benefit" initiatives — for example, migrating legacy systems to cloud reduces both cost (infrastructure savings) and enables innovation (cloud-native capabilities). Use business capability modeling to show which capabilities need cost optimization and which need innovation investment, demonstrating that they largely affect different parts of the business. Include both the $60 million savings target and the $80 million innovation investment in the vision, showing they are not mutually exclusive when mapped to different capability domains.

d) Engage the CEO as the executive sponsor to arbitrate between the CFO and CTO before developing the Architecture Vision. TOGAF requires that the Architecture Vision align with the business strategy, and the conflicting stakeholder positions represent an unresolved business strategy question. The architect cannot develop a credible Architecture Vision without clear strategic direction. Request a strategy workshop facilitated by the CEO where the firm's strategic priorities are explicitly ranked.

## Scenario 3: Cloud Migration Blocked by Data Sovereignty Concerns

MediCore Health Systems is a healthcare company operating in 8 countries, managing electronic health records for 45 million patients and processing $12 billion in insurance claims annually. The enterprise architecture team has completed Phases A through C and is now in Phase D (Technology Architecture). The target Technology Architecture recommends migrating 80% of the company's infrastructure to a public cloud platform to reduce costs by $35 million annually, improve disaster recovery capabilities, and enable advanced analytics for population health management.

The Chief Information Security Officer (CISO), Dr. Kenji Tanaka, has formally objected to the cloud migration, citing multiple concerns. First, patient data is subject to different sovereignty requirements in each of the 8 countries — some require data to remain within national borders, others require specific encryption standards, and two countries have not yet clarified their position on cloud storage of health data. Second, a recent ransomware attack on a competitor's cloud infrastructure resulted in 2 million patient records being exposed. Third, MediCore's existing HIPAA compliance framework was designed for on-premises infrastructure and would need to be completely redesigned for cloud. Fourth, Dr. Tanaka's security team of 25 has no cloud security expertise and would need 12-18 months of training and hiring to develop this capability.

The CIO supports the cloud migration and has the CEO's backing. The board of directors is divided. The Architecture Board has asked the enterprise architect to present a recommendation at the next meeting. The $35 million in annual savings is critical — the company is under pressure from shareholders to improve margins. However, a data breach involving patient records could result in regulatory penalties exceeding $500 million across all jurisdictions, not to mention reputational damage.

### Question 3
Based on the scenario above, how should the enterprise architect proceed?

a) Revise the Phase D Technology Architecture to adopt a hybrid cloud model with a data sovereignty framework. Classify all data assets by sensitivity and jurisdictional requirements. Design the architecture so that patient data remains in country-specific environments (on-premises or sovereign cloud regions) while non-sensitive workloads (analytics, development, corporate systems) migrate to public cloud. Create a security architecture overlay that maps cloud security controls to each country's requirements. Present this to the Architecture Board as a revised target architecture that achieves 60-70% of the cost savings while addressing all sovereignty concerns.

b) Support the CISO's objection and revise the target Technology Architecture to recommend a private cloud / on-premises modernization instead. The architect's responsibility is to ensure the architecture is fit for purpose, and a $500 million+ regulatory risk clearly outweighs $35 million in annual savings. Recommend investing the savings target in infrastructure modernization and automation that achieves some cost reduction without the sovereignty and security risks. Present this to the Architecture Board with a clear risk analysis.

c) Maintain the original public cloud recommendation but add a comprehensive risk mitigation plan as a companion document. Include: a country-by-country data sovereignty compliance matrix, a cloud security architecture designed by an external specialist firm, a 12-month security team upskilling program, and contractual requirements for the cloud provider addressing data residency and encryption. Present this to the Architecture Board with a clear risk-benefit analysis showing that the $35 million savings and advanced analytics capabilities outweigh the manageable residual risks.

d) Recommend that the Architecture Board defer the Technology Architecture decision by 6 months to allow for: resolution of regulatory uncertainty in the two ambiguous countries, completion of a formal security assessment of the proposed cloud platform, development of a cloud security capability within the CISO's team, and creation of a proof-of-concept migration using non-sensitive data. Use this period to gather evidence that reduces the decision's uncertainty while continuing with Phase D work on non-cloud aspects of the Technology Architecture.

## Scenario 4: Recovering from Phase H Neglect

Continental Logistics is a global supply chain company operating in 30 countries with 60,000 employees. Five years ago, the company invested $8 million in establishing an enterprise architecture practice and successfully completed three full ADM cycles, producing a comprehensive enterprise architecture covering business, data, application, and technology domains. The architecture was widely praised and guided $200 million in successful technology investments.

However, the company never properly implemented Phase H (Architecture Change Management). The original Chief Architect who championed the EA practice left the company 3 years ago, and her replacement focused on new project architecture rather than maintaining the enterprise architecture. No formal change management process was established, no architecture monitoring was conducted, and no drivers for change were systematically assessed.

The result is that the Architecture Repository is now severely outdated. The Business Architecture describes an organizational structure that was reorganized 18 months ago. The Application Architecture references 30 applications that have been decommissioned and doesn't include 45 applications that have been introduced. The Technology Architecture still shows the previous on-premises data center, which was closed after a cloud migration 2 years ago. Architecture principles established 5 years ago reference market conditions that no longer apply. The Standards Information Base references technology standards that are end-of-life.

A new CEO has arrived and wants to use the enterprise architecture to guide a major strategic pivot into e-commerce logistics. When she was shown the Architecture Repository, she described it as "a museum exhibit, not a management tool." The Architecture Board is demoralized, stakeholders have lost confidence in the EA team, and two business units have started developing their own architectures independently. The EA team has 6 architects and a mandate to recover the enterprise architecture within 9 months.

### Question 4
Based on the scenario above, what is the best recovery strategy?

a) Restart the ADM from Phase A with the new CEO's e-commerce logistics strategy as the driver. Use the current state as an opportunity to build a completely fresh baseline architecture that reflects the organization as it is today. Don't attempt to update the old architecture — instead, treat this as a new Architecture Vision engagement. The outdated repository content can serve as historical reference but should not be treated as authoritative. Establish Phase H processes as an explicit deliverable of this ADM cycle.

b) First, establish the Phase H processes that were never implemented: create an architecture change management framework, define triggers for architecture review, establish monitoring mechanisms, and assign architecture stewards for each domain. Then conduct a "rapid re-baselining" exercise: update the Architecture Landscape in the repository to reflect current state across all domains within 3 months. Only after the baseline is current, launch a new ADM cycle (Phase A) for the e-commerce logistics strategy. This ensures the next cycle starts from an accurate foundation.

c) Focus first on rebuilding stakeholder confidence and EA credibility. Conduct an Architecture Maturity Assessment to objectively demonstrate the current state. Work with the two business units doing independent architecture to integrate their work into the enterprise architecture. Deliver quick architectural wins — such as reference architectures for common patterns or architecture reviews of active projects — within 90 days. Simultaneously update the most critical repository sections. Begin the e-commerce logistics ADM cycle once credibility is restored (approximately month 4-5).

d) Commission the EA team to update all four architecture domains (Business, Data, Application, Technology) simultaneously over 6 months, bringing the Architecture Repository fully current. Assign each of the 6 architects to a specific domain or cross-cutting concern. Establish proper Phase H processes during this update exercise. Once the repository is current, present the refreshed architecture to the new CEO and use it as the baseline for the e-commerce logistics strategy ADM cycle.

## Scenario 5: Integrating TOGAF ADM with SAFe Agile Framework

DigitalFirst Insurance is a $10 billion insurer undergoing a multi-year digital transformation. The company has a mature TOGAF-based enterprise architecture practice with 15 architects. Two years ago, the IT delivery organization adopted the Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe) for all software development, with 25 Agile Release Trains (ARTs) delivering software in 10-week Program Increments (PIs). The transformation has created a growing tension between the EA team and the delivery organization.

The EA team operates on traditional TOGAF ADM timelines — their current ADM cycle for the claims modernization initiative has been running for 8 months and is currently in Phase D. The delivery ARTs, meanwhile, have already begun building components based on early Phase B outputs because SAFe requires continuous delivery. The ART teams complain that the architecture is "always lagging behind implementation" and that Architecture Board approvals take weeks when they need decisions in days. The EA team counters that the ARTs are making technology decisions without proper architectural oversight, creating fragmentation and technical debt.

Specific conflicts include: three ARTs have selected different API gateway technologies because the Application Architecture hadn't been completed when they needed to make the decision. Two ARTs building the same customer data model have taken different approaches because the Data Architecture was still in development. The SAFe Release Train Engineer (RTE) for the claims domain has threatened to "stop inviting architects to PI Planning" if they continue to "slow things down." The CIO has tasked the Chief Architect with developing an integrated operating model that preserves architectural governance while enabling agile delivery speed. The CIO has made it clear that neither the EA practice nor SAFe adoption will be abandoned.

### Question 5
Based on the scenario above, how should the Chief Architect integrate the ADM with the agile framework?

a) Restructure the ADM to operate on SAFe cadences. Break each ADM phase into increments that align with the 10-week PI cycle. Produce "Minimum Viable Architecture" outputs at each PI boundary that provide just enough architectural guidance for the next PI's implementation work. Embed architects directly in ARTs as System Architects to provide real-time architectural decisions. Maintain the Architecture Board for strategic decisions but delegate tactical architecture decisions to the embedded architects. Use PI Planning events as architecture review checkpoints.

b) Establish a two-speed architecture model: a "Strategic Architecture" track that follows the traditional ADM timeline for enterprise-level architectural decisions (principles, standards, reference architectures, capability roadmaps) and a "Solution Architecture" track that operates at ART speed, making tactical decisions within guardrails set by the Strategic Architecture. Define clear decision rights: what the Architecture Board decides (strategic) versus what embedded architects decide (tactical). Create an Architecture Runway concept aligned with SAFe that ensures architecture work runs 1-2 PIs ahead of delivery.

c) Mandate that all ARTs freeze development decisions until the current ADM cycle completes Phase D. The current fragmentation (different API gateways, inconsistent data models) validates the EA team's concerns and demonstrates why architecture must precede implementation. Present the technical debt analysis to the CIO showing the cost of the fragmentation. Once the ADM cycle is complete, provide comprehensive architecture artifacts that the ARTs must follow. Implement architecture compliance reviews at every PI boundary.

d) Adopt SAFe's built-in "Agile Architecture" practices and retire the separate TOGAF ADM process. SAFe already includes Architectural Runway, Enabler Epics, and System Architect roles that provide architecture governance within the agile framework. Map TOGAF architecture artifacts to SAFe artifacts (e.g., Architecture Vision becomes a Solution Intent, architecture principles become Architectural Guardrails). This eliminates the two-culture problem by fully integrating architecture into the agile delivery framework.

## Scenario 6: Negative ROI Architecture Roadmap

Atlas Energy Corporation is a diversified energy company ($22 billion revenue) transitioning from fossil fuels to renewable energy. The enterprise architecture team has completed Phase E (Opportunities and Solutions) and Phase F (Migration Planning) for a comprehensive IT transformation to support the business transition. The architecture roadmap is ambitious: it includes replacing the core energy trading platform, building a new renewable energy asset management system, modernizing the grid operations technology, implementing advanced analytics for energy forecasting, and deploying IoT infrastructure for smart grid management.

During Phase F, the financial analysis reveals uncomfortable numbers. The total transformation cost is estimated at $450 million over 5 years. The projected benefits — including operational efficiency gains, reduced technology maintenance costs, improved trading performance, and new revenue from data services — total $380 million over the same 5-year period. The Net Present Value (NPV) at the company's 12% discount rate is negative $95 million. The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is 7.2%, well below the company's 15% hurdle rate. The first 3 years show pure cost with benefits only materializing in years 4 and 5.

The CFO has received a draft of the cost-benefit analysis and has called an emergency meeting with the Architecture Board. She states: "I cannot present a negative ROI program to the board of directors. Either fix the numbers or kill the program." The Chief Architect knows the numbers are accurate. The CTO argues that the transformation is "existential — without it, we can't compete in the renewable energy market." The CEO has publicly committed to the renewable energy transition in the company's annual report. The 15-member Architecture Board is divided: 8 members support continuing, 7 want to restructure the program.

### Question 6
Based on the scenario above, how should the Chief Architect present the architecture roadmap to stakeholders?

a) Restructure the architecture roadmap into two phases with separate business cases. Phase 1 (Years 1-3, $180 million): focus on the trading platform and analytics capabilities that deliver measurable financial returns. Phase 2 (Years 3-5, $270 million): grid modernization and IoT infrastructure, with Phase 2 funding contingent on Phase 1 delivering projected benefits. Create Transition Architectures that deliver independent value at each phase boundary. Present Phase 1 with a positive ROI while acknowledging that Phase 2 will require a future business case validated by Phase 1 results.

b) Challenge the financial analysis methodology. Enterprise architecture transformations create strategic value that isn't captured in traditional NPV/IRR analysis. Develop a balanced scorecard approach that includes: strategic alignment value (enabling the renewable energy transition), risk reduction value (avoiding obsolescence of current systems), organizational capability value (building digital competency), and option value (creating technology platforms that enable future revenue streams not yet identified). Present this expanded value framework alongside the traditional financial analysis.

c) Accept the negative ROI as accurate and present it honestly to the Architecture Board with a clear recommendation: the transformation is strategically necessary despite the negative financial return within the 5-year window. Extend the analysis to a 10-year horizon where the benefits compound significantly. Include a risk analysis showing the cost of NOT transforming (loss of competitive position, regulatory non-compliance, stranded technology assets). Frame this as a strategic investment decision, not an IT project decision, and recommend the CEO present it to the board of directors in that context.

d) Redesign the architecture roadmap to achieve a positive ROI within the 5-year window. This requires reducing scope: prioritize the components with the highest financial return and defer or eliminate those with the lowest return. Use TOGAF's gap analysis to identify which Transition Architectures deliver the most benefit relative to cost. Accept that the full target architecture cannot be achieved within the current financial constraints and document the deferred capabilities for a future ADM cycle. Present a roadmap that the CFO can confidently take to the board.

## Scenario 7: Undocumented Architecture Dispensations

SafeGuard Insurance is a national insurer with $8 billion in premiums and 12,000 employees. The company has operated a TOGAF-based architecture governance program for 4 years. A quarterly compliance audit by the internal audit department has uncovered a serious governance failure: over the past 18 months, 23 architecture dispensations have been granted to various projects, but only 5 have proper documentation in the Governance Log.

Investigating further, the Chief Architect discovers that: 8 dispensations were granted verbally by the previous Architecture Board chair (who has since left the company) with no written record. 6 dispensations were granted via email between project managers and individual architects, bypassing the Architecture Board entirely. 4 dispensations were "self-granted" by project teams who interpreted ambiguous architecture standards as permission to deviate. The remaining 5 are properly documented with formal dispensation requests, impact assessments, time limits, and remediation plans.

The impact is significant: the 18 improperly granted dispensations have introduced technology diversity that increases operational costs by an estimated $4 million annually. Three of the dispensations involved security-related deviations that the CISO was never informed about. Two dispensations conflict with each other — one project was given permission to use technology X while another was told technology X was not permitted. The internal audit report will go to the board's Risk Committee, and the Chief Architect must present a remediation plan. The CIO is concerned about the reputational damage to the architecture program and wants "this fixed quietly." Several project teams are pushing back, arguing that their dispensations were legitimately granted and they shouldn't be required to change now.

### Question 7
Based on the scenario above, how should the Chief Architect address the dispensation documentation failure?

a) Conduct a retrospective review of all 23 dispensations. For the 5 properly documented ones, validate that remediation plans are being followed. For the 18 undocumented ones, require each project to submit a formal dispensation request retroactively, including impact assessment and remediation plan. The Architecture Board should review each retroactive request on its merits and either formalize the dispensation with conditions or require the project to remediate the deviation. Simultaneously, strengthen the governance process: update the dispensation procedure to require written requests, Architecture Board review, impact assessment, time limits, and CISO notification for security-related deviations. Present this as both a correction and an improvement to the board's Risk Committee.

b) Accept that the 18 dispensations were granted in good faith (even if improperly) and retroactively formalize all of them with current-dated documentation. Requiring projects to submit retroactive requests or remediate deviations that were granted permission (even informally) would undermine trust in the governance process and punish project teams for the Architecture Board's governance failure. Focus all effort on fixing the process going forward: implement a dispensation management system, require all future dispensations to follow the proper process, and establish quarterly dispensation audits.

c) Classify the 18 undocumented dispensations by risk level. Immediately address the 3 security-related dispensations by engaging the CISO and conducting security assessments — these represent organizational risk regardless of governance process. For the remaining 15, create a prioritized remediation roadmap based on cost and risk impact. Revoke dispensations that conflict with each other and require those projects to align. Reform the governance process to prevent recurrence. Present to the Risk Committee a transparent accounting of the failures, their risk implications, and the remediation plan with timelines.

d) Commission an independent audit of the entire architecture governance program, not just dispensations. The dispensation failure is likely symptomatic of broader governance weaknesses. The audit should assess: Architecture Board effectiveness, compliance review processes, architecture decision documentation practices, and stakeholder understanding of governance responsibilities. Use the audit findings to redesign the governance framework comprehensively rather than just fixing the dispensation process. Present the comprehensive review plan to the Risk Committee as evidence of taking the issue seriously.

## Scenario 8: Rapid Acquisition Integration Under Time Pressure

QuickServe Restaurants is a fast-casual dining chain with 2,500 locations across North America. The company has just acquired FreshBowl, a smaller but rapidly growing health-focused restaurant chain with 400 locations. The acquisition was driven by QuickServe's need to enter the health-conscious dining market. The CEO has mandated "full technology integration within 6 months" to achieve $30 million in annual synergies from shared supply chain, POS systems, loyalty programs, and back-office operations.

QuickServe has a mature TOGAF-based enterprise architecture with a well-populated Architecture Repository. FreshBowl has no formal architecture — their technology was built opportunistically by a small IT team of 15 people. FreshBowl's systems include a custom-built POS system (their CTO's pride), a third-party supply chain management tool, a basic loyalty program run on a marketing SaaS platform, and financial systems running on QuickBooks. None of these systems were designed for the scale QuickServe operates at.

The enterprise architecture team at QuickServe (8 architects) must decide how to approach this integration. Complications include: FreshBowl's custom POS system has features that QuickServe's standard POS lacks (specifically, complex nutritional tracking and ingredient substitution logic that health-conscious customers demand). FreshBowl's CTO is staying on post-acquisition and is emotionally attached to the custom POS. QuickServe's Architecture Board has a standard principle: "All acquisitions adopt the QuickServe technology standard." However, the QuickServe POS vendor has quoted 18 months to add the nutritional tracking features. The 6-month timeline means some compromises are necessary. The supply chain integration is straightforward but the POS integration is contentious.

### Question 8
Based on the scenario above, how should the enterprise architecture team adapt the ADM for this rapid acquisition integration?

a) Execute a compressed ADM cycle with a strict 6-month timeline. Conduct a 2-week Preliminary Phase to understand FreshBowl's architecture landscape. Spend 2 weeks on Phase A creating an Architecture Vision for the integration. Accelerate through Phases B-D in 6 weeks using the QuickServe architecture as the target and FreshBowl as the baseline, identifying gaps. Use Phase E to create work packages with aggressive timelines. For the POS conflict, grant a temporary dispensation allowing FreshBowl to keep their POS for 18 months until QuickServe's vendor delivers the nutritional features, with a defined migration plan. Integrate all other systems on the QuickServe standard within 6 months.

b) Apply the ADM's iteration capability to run two parallel architecture streams. Stream 1 (months 1-6): focus on the "quick win" integrations — supply chain, back-office, financial systems, and loyalty program — using QuickServe's existing architecture as the target. Stream 2 (months 1-12): conduct a proper ADM cycle for the POS integration, using Phase B to understand the business requirements FreshBowl's POS serves and Phase D to determine the right long-term technology approach. Explain to the CEO that full integration requires 12 months, not 6, with the POS stream being the critical path.

c) Recommend that the Architecture Board waive its "all acquisitions adopt QuickServe standards" principle for this acquisition. FreshBowl's unique nutritional tracking capability is a competitive differentiator that drove the acquisition. Instead, reverse-integrate: adopt FreshBowl's POS as the standard for health-focused locations and create an integration architecture that connects it to QuickServe's enterprise systems. Use the Enterprise Continuum to classify FreshBowl's POS features as Organization-Specific building blocks that extend the QuickServe architecture.

d) Follow the standard QuickServe acquisition playbook: migrate all FreshBowl systems to QuickServe standards within 6 months as the Architecture Board principle requires. For the POS nutritional tracking gap, implement a temporary workaround using QuickServe's POS with a bolt-on module developed by the IT team. While not as feature-rich as FreshBowl's custom POS, this approach maintains architectural consistency, meets the CEO's timeline, and avoids the complexity of maintaining two POS platforms. Address the FreshBowl CTO's concerns through a dedicated change management program.

## Answer Key

### Scenario 1 - Question 1
**Scoring:**
- **Answer b) = 5 points (Best):** This approach correctly recognizes that architecture capability must precede architecture practice. TOGAF's guidance on establishing an Architecture Capability (ADM Phase Preliminary and the Architecture Capability Framework) emphasizes that the organization must have foundational elements — skills, processes, governance, stakeholder buy-in — before the ADM can operate effectively. At Maturity Level 1, launching a formal ADM cycle immediately would fail because the organizational preconditions don't exist. The 6-month capability building phase addresses skills, relationships (critical given CIO resistance and PMO competition), lightweight governance, and stakeholder education. Beginning a formal ADM in month 7 with a well-scoped Segment Architecture is realistic and demonstrates ADM operation. Presenting a maturity progression plan manages the CEO's expectations honestly.
- **Answer a) = 3 points (Second):** This approach is methodologically correct — the Preliminary Phase is indeed the right starting point — and completing one ADM cycle in 12 months is ambitious but theoretically possible. However, it underestimates the organizational readiness challenge. The Preliminary Phase requires stakeholder engagement, principles agreement, and governance establishment, which are difficult to achieve in 3 months when the organization has no architecture awareness. Reporting the ADM as "operational" after one project cycle is technically accurate but potentially misleading. The approach could work if the organizational culture were more receptive.
- **Answer d) = 1 point (Third):** Resetting expectations with the CEO is honest and the Architecture Maturity Model is the right analytical tool. However, directly contradicting the CEO's public commitment is politically risky and may result in the EA program being seen as an obstacle rather than an enabler. Embedding architects in projects is a good tactical move but isn't connected to a structured ADM deployment. Prioritizing the Repository and Standards Information Base before establishing governance and stakeholder relationships misorders the priorities for a Level 1 organization.
- **Answer c) = 0 points (Distractor):** Creating a "simplified ADM" that skips Business and Technology Architecture phases fundamentally undermines the ADM's value. The ADM phases exist because each contributes essential architectural analysis — skipping them doesn't simplify the ADM, it breaks it. Applying even a simplified process across five projects simultaneously with a 4-person team is impractical. Reporting to the CEO that the ADM is "operational" when only a fraction of it is being used is misleading and will eventually be exposed, damaging the EA practice's credibility permanently.

### Scenario 2 - Question 2
**Scoring:**
- **Answer c) = 5 points (Best):** This approach best demonstrates the enterprise architect's role in creating a coherent Architecture Vision from apparently conflicting requirements. Business capability modeling — a core TOGAF technique — can objectively identify which capabilities are cost optimization targets (mature, stable capabilities with redundant implementations) and which are innovation targets (emerging capabilities requiring new technology). The 180-application portfolio likely contains both categories. By mapping the CFO's consolidation targets and the CTO's innovation investments to different capability domains, the architect shows they aren't competing for the same resources. Including both financial targets in the vision is bold but defensible if the capability analysis supports it. This approach fulfills the architect's responsibility to find architectural solutions to business problems.
- **Answer a) = 3 points (Second):** The "Rationalize to Innovate" narrative is compelling and provides a clear sequencing logic. Using decommissioning savings to fund innovation investment is a proven enterprise architecture value proposition. However, the strict Year 1/Year 2 sequencing may not be acceptable to the CTO, who has board support for innovation and won't want to wait 12 months. The separate stakeholder validation approach is good stakeholder management but risks being seen as political manipulation if the stakeholders discover the architect is managing them separately. The approach is sound but creates an artificial either/or sequencing that may not be necessary.
- **Answer d) = 1 point (Third):** Escalating to the CEO is premature. TOGAF positions the enterprise architect as the professional who synthesizes stakeholder views into architectural solutions — escalating a stakeholder conflict to the executive sponsor before attempting a synthesis fails to fulfill this role. The CEO has already indicated he expects Maria to resolve this ("find a way to make it work").
