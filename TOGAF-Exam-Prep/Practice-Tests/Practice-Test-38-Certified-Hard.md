## Scenario 1: Capability Gap with No Market Solution

Meridian Insurance Group is a large property and casualty insurer ($18 billion in premiums) operating in 12 countries. The enterprise architecture team has completed Phase B (Business Architecture) and Phase C (Information Systems Architecture) for a strategic initiative to transform the company's claims processing operation. The gap analysis conducted between the baseline and target Business Architectures has revealed a critical finding: the target Business Architecture requires an "Automated Multi-Jurisdiction Claims Adjudication" capability that can simultaneously apply claims rules from 12 different regulatory jurisdictions, incorporate real-time catastrophe modeling data, and execute automated settlement decisions up to $500,000 per claim.

The enterprise architect's research reveals that this capability does not exist in any of Meridian's current systems, nor is it available as a commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) product from any vendor. Three enterprise software vendors offer claims management platforms, but none support automated adjudication across multiple jurisdictions simultaneously — they all require sequential, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction processing. The closest comparable capability exists at a competitor, but it was custom-built over 4 years at an estimated cost of $120 million and is proprietary.

The CTO wants to build the capability in-house using AI/ML technology, estimating $60 million and 3 years. The CFO argues this is too expensive and too risky — the company should redesign the target Business Architecture to work with sequential jurisdiction processing. The Chief Actuary warns that without automated multi-jurisdiction adjudication, the company cannot meet its target of reducing claims settlement time from 45 days to 5 days, which is the key business driver. The Architecture Board is meeting to decide the approach. Meanwhile, two of the three vendor platforms are being evaluated for the broader claims platform — choosing one would constrain future automation options.

### Question 1
Based on the scenario above, what approach should the enterprise architect recommend?

a) Revise the target Business Architecture to accommodate sequential jurisdiction processing, as the CFO recommends. The TOGAF ADM is iterative — if the target architecture requires a capability that doesn't exist and cannot be reasonably built, the target should be adjusted to reflect reality. Create a Transition Architecture that uses sequential processing initially with a defined architectural evolution path toward automated multi-jurisdiction processing as technology matures. Include this as an architectural principle: "design for current capability with clear extension points."

b) Recommend a phased approach: first, select one of the three vendor platforms based on Phase D analysis (Technology Architecture) for core claims management. Second, develop a custom "Adjudication Engine" as a separate architectural component using API-based integration, designed to eventually support multi-jurisdiction automation. Third, create a Transition Architecture that delivers sequential processing in Phase 1 (12 months) and adds multi-jurisdiction automation incrementally in Phase 2 (24 months) as AI/ML capabilities are developed. Use Architecture Building Blocks to define the Adjudication Engine interface so it can evolve independently from the vendor platform.

c) Support the CTO's recommendation to build the capability in-house. The gap analysis has identified a strategic capability that differentiates Meridian from competitors. TOGAF's Enterprise Continuum classifies this as an Organization-Specific Architecture — it cannot be sourced from Foundation, Common Systems, or Industry levels. The $60 million investment is justified by the competitive advantage. Recommend that the Architecture Board approve an in-house build with an ADM cycle specifically for the Adjudication Engine capability.

d) Pause the claims transformation initiative and commission a broader market study and technology assessment before proceeding. The gap analysis has revealed a fundamental uncertainty — the required capability may or may not be achievable with current technology. Before committing to any approach, conduct a proof of concept with the CTO's AI/ML approach, evaluate whether any of the three vendor platforms could be extended, and assess whether the business target (45 to 5 days) can be partially achieved with alternative approaches. Present the findings to the Architecture Board in 3 months.

## Scenario 2: Consolidating Three Architecture Frameworks

Universal Media Group is a global entertainment conglomerate ($30 billion revenue) formed through successive acquisitions over 15 years. The conglomerate consists of three major divisions: StreamVision (streaming and digital content, $12 billion), ClassicStudios (film and television production, $10 billion), and TuneWave (music recording and distribution, $8 billion). Each division has independently established its own enterprise architecture practice.

StreamVision uses TOGAF with a strong technology focus, maintaining a comprehensive Technology Reference Model and Architecture Repository with over 5,000 building blocks. ClassicStudios adopted the Zachman Framework 8 years ago and has organized its architecture knowledge into a detailed 6x6 matrix covering all stakeholder perspectives. TuneWave uses the Federal Enterprise Architecture Framework (FEAF) adapted for their industry, with strong Business Reference Models and performance measurement frameworks. Each division has its own Architecture Board, architecture principles, and governance processes.

The new CEO, appointed 6 months ago, has mandated "One Universal Architecture" to enable cross-divisional synergies worth $500 million annually. These synergies include: a unified content management platform, shared customer identity and data analytics, consolidated technology infrastructure, and integrated supply chain (content acquisition, production, distribution). The CEO has appointed Universal's first Chief Enterprise Architect, Dr. Amara Osei, to lead the consolidation. Dr. Osei has 6 months to present a unified architecture governance model to the board.

Challenges include: each division's architects believe their framework is superior. StreamVision's CTO (TOGAF advocate) has 180 architects; ClassicStudios' VP of Architecture (Zachman advocate) has 90 architects; TuneWave's Architecture Director (FEAF advocate) has 45 architects. The 315 architects collectively represent $75 million in annual personnel costs. The CEO has stated that the unified architecture team should be no larger than 200 architects, implying significant staff reductions.

### Question 2
Based on the scenario above, how should Dr. Osei consolidate the three architecture frameworks?

a) Adopt TOGAF as the unified framework since StreamVision (the largest division by revenue and architect count) already uses it. Transition ClassicStudios' Zachman artifacts and TuneWave's FEAF artifacts to TOGAF equivalents. Map Zachman's 6x6 matrix perspectives to TOGAF's ADM phases and Architecture Content Framework. Map FEAF's Business Reference Models to TOGAF's Business Architecture. Establish a single Architecture Board using TOGAF governance. This approach leverages the largest existing investment and community.

b) Create a "Universal Architecture Method" that synthesizes the best elements from all three frameworks. Adopt TOGAF's ADM as the process framework, Zachman's multi-perspective classification scheme as the architecture content organization model, and FEAF's Business Reference Models and performance measurement approach for business architecture and EA value measurement. Create a unified Architecture Content Metamodel that maps to all three frameworks. Establish a new Architecture Board with representation from all divisions. This preserves the strengths of each framework and respects each division's contribution.

c) Use TOGAF's Architecture Partitioning approach to establish a federated model. Create a Strategic Architecture at the enterprise level using TOGAF (since it provides the most comprehensive process framework) that defines shared principles, standards, and cross-divisional integration patterns. Allow each division to maintain their current framework for Segment Architectures, provided they deliver architecture outputs that conform to the Strategic Architecture's integration requirements. Create a mapping between each framework's outputs and the enterprise-level architecture content requirements. Establish a federated Architecture Board with enterprise-level and division-level governance tiers.

d) Commission an independent assessment of all three frameworks' effectiveness within their respective divisions before deciding on a consolidation approach. Evaluate: which framework has produced the best business outcomes, which has the most reusable artifacts, which best supports the cross-divisional synergy goals, and which has the highest architect satisfaction and adoption. Use this evidence to select the framework that objectively best serves Universal's needs. This data-driven approach avoids political bias and ensures the best framework wins.

## Scenario 3: Defending the EA Program from Executive Budget Cuts

Velocity Telecommunications is a major telecom operator ($25 billion revenue) with 50,000 employees serving 30 million customers. The enterprise architecture program has been operating for 6 years with a team of 20 architects and an annual budget of $6 million. Over this period, the team has delivered three complete ADM cycles, established an Architecture Repository with comprehensive business, data, application, and technology architectures, and provided architecture governance for $2 billion in technology investments.

However, a new CEO, Robert Kaine, has been appointed with a mandate from the board to cut costs and improve profitability. Robert comes from a private equity background with no technology or architecture experience. In his first 90 days, he has questioned every corporate function's value proposition. He has now turned to the EA program, stating in an executive meeting: "I see a team of 20 people who produce documents that nobody outside IT reads. Our competitors don't have enterprise architects, and they seem to do fine. I'm cutting this program's budget by 75% — you can keep 5 people to maintain the repository, or you can show me the ROI of architecture."

The Chief Architect, Dr. Marcus Holt, has 4 weeks to present a defense. He knows that the EA program has contributed to significant value: architecture governance identified and prevented 3 potential project failures worth $150 million in averted losses, the Architecture Repository has reduced new project startup time by 40%, and architecture standards have reduced technology diversity from 200+ technology products to 85, saving an estimated $15 million annually in licensing and support costs. However, much of this value is difficult to attribute directly to the EA program because projects, operations, and vendors also contributed. The CIO supports the EA program but has been told by Robert to "stay out of this — this is about corporate efficiency, not IT empire building."

### Question 3
Based on the scenario above, how should Dr. Holt respond to defend the EA program?

a) Prepare a comprehensive ROI analysis using TOGAF's guidance on EA value measurement. Quantify the direct value: $150 million in averted project failures (attribute 50% to architecture governance = $75 million), $15 million annual licensing savings, 40% reduction in project startup time (quantified as $8 million annual savings). Calculate the EA program's ROI as the total value delivered ($98 million) against the total cost ($36 million over 6 years) = 272% ROI. Present this to the CEO with case studies showing specific instances where architecture governance caught problems before they became expensive.

b) Acknowledge the CEO's perspective and propose restructuring the EA program to directly demonstrate business value. Transition from a documentation-focused model to a "value delivery" model: reduce the team to 12 architects, embed them in the company's top strategic programs as architecture advisors, eliminate stand-alone architecture documentation in favor of architecture deliverables that are integrated into project artifacts, and establish quarterly EA value reports that track specific metrics the CEO cares about (cost savings, project success rates, time-to-market). Frame this as an evolution, not a defense.

c) Request that the CEO defer the budget cut for 6 months while the EA team demonstrates its value through a specific high-visibility engagement. Identify the company's most critical upcoming technology decision and offer to apply the full ADM to deliver measurable results. If the EA team delivers demonstrable value within 6 months, the program continues with a restructured mandate. If not, accept the budget reduction. This "prove it or lose it" approach demonstrates confidence and gives the CEO concrete evidence.

d) Escalate to the board of directors' Technology Committee, presenting the architecture program's track record and the risks of dismantling it. TOGAF's governance framework positions enterprise architecture as a board-level concern because it manages technology investment risk and strategic alignment. The CEO's proposal to cut the program based on a superficial assessment threatens the organization's ability to govern $2 billion in technology investments. The Technology Committee should be the decision-making body for a change this consequential.

## Scenario 4: Major Deviation Request During Implementation Governance

Apex Financial Technologies is a fintech company ($4 billion revenue) that provides payment processing infrastructure to 10,000 merchants worldwide. The company is executing a major platform modernization program guided by a TOGAF-based target architecture approved 18 months ago. The target architecture specifies an event-driven microservices architecture with eventual consistency, deployed on Kubernetes across three cloud regions, using Apache Kafka for event streaming and PostgreSQL for persistent storage.

During Phase G (Implementation Governance), Project Mercury — the highest-priority implementation project responsible for building the new real-time payment processing engine — has submitted a major deviation request. The project's technical lead argues that the target architecture's eventual consistency model is inappropriate for real-time payment processing, which requires immediate consistency to prevent double-charging merchants. The project team wants to: (1) replace Kafka with a custom message broker that guarantees exactly-once delivery, (2) use a distributed SQL database (CockroachDB) instead of PostgreSQL for strong consistency across regions, and (3) implement a synchronous API pattern instead of the event-driven approach for payment transactions.

The deviation request is technically sophisticated and well-argued. However, three other implementation projects (Projects Venus, Mars, and Jupiter) are building components that integrate with Mercury and are designed for the approved event-driven, eventually consistent architecture. Accepting Mercury's deviations would require Venus, Mars, and Jupiter to redesign their integration patterns, adding an estimated $8 million in costs and 4 months of delay. Rejecting the deviation could result in a payment processing system that occasionally double-charges merchants — a regulatory violation that could cost the company its payment processing license.

The Architecture Board must decide within 2 weeks because Mercury's development sprint is blocked pending the decision.

### Question 4
Based on the scenario above, what should the Architecture Board's response be?

a) Grant the deviation for Project Mercury and accept the cascading impact on Projects Venus, Mars, and Jupiter. The payment processing domain has legitimately different architectural requirements than the other projects, and the regulatory risk of double-charging merchants is an existential threat to the business. Document this as a formal architecture dispensation with a clear scope: the synchronous consistency pattern applies ONLY to the payment transaction critical path, while ancillary payment functions (reporting, notifications, reconciliation) remain on the approved event-driven pattern. Require Mercury to maintain Kafka integration points for downstream consumers.

b) Reject the deviation and require Project Mercury to find a solution within the approved architecture. Event-driven architectures with eventual consistency are widely used in payment processing (Stripe, Square, and others use similar patterns). The project team's concern about double-charging can be addressed through idempotency keys, compensating transactions, and saga patterns — all of which are compatible with the approved architecture. Commission an architecture spike to develop the exact-once payment processing pattern within the approved architecture, and share the pattern as a building block for other projects.

c) Convene an emergency Architecture Review to reassess the target architecture for the payment processing domain. The deviation request reveals a potential gap in the original Phase D Technology Architecture — the implications of eventual consistency for payment processing may not have been adequately analyzed. Rather than granting or denying the deviation, commission a focused Phase D iteration specifically for the payment processing domain. This 4-week analysis should determine whether the target architecture needs to be revised for payment-critical paths, with the revision applying to all affected projects simultaneously rather than as a single-project dispensation.

d) Propose a compromise: allow Mercury to use CockroachDB for the payment transaction database (addressing the consistency concern) but require it to maintain the Kafka event-driven integration pattern for cross-project communication. This preserves the enterprise integration architecture while allowing domain-specific technology selection where the requirements justify it. Document this as a limited dispensation with a review point after 6 months to assess whether the hybrid approach should be formalized in the target architecture.

## Scenario 5: Big-Bang vs. Strangler Fig Transition Architecture

Fortuna Banking Corporation is a century-old banking institution ($50 billion in assets) that operates a monolithic core banking platform built in COBOL on mainframes. The platform processes all of the bank's operations: deposit accounts, lending, payments, trade finance, and treasury management. The system has been continuously modified for 35 years and now contains 12 million lines of code. The bank has completed Phases A through D of the ADM, and the target architecture specifies a modern cloud-native microservices platform.

Phase E (Opportunities and Solutions) has identified two competing Transition Architecture approaches, and the Architecture Board must choose between them.

**Option 1: Big-Bang Replacement ("Project Phoenix").** Build the entire new platform in parallel with the existing mainframe over 30 months, then execute a single cutover weekend. Estimated cost: $200 million. The advantage is a clean break from legacy technology and a single, definitive migration. The risk is catastrophic: if the cutover fails, the bank cannot process transactions, potentially causing a banking crisis. The bank's regulator has expressed concern about this approach. Industry data shows that 60% of big-bang banking migrations experience significant issues.

**Option 2: Strangler Fig Pattern ("Project Evolution").** Incrementally replace mainframe functions with microservices over 48 months, routing transactions through a facade layer that progressively directs traffic to new services. Estimated cost: $280 million. The advantage is lower risk — each function is migrated independently and can be rolled back. The disadvantage is 18 months longer, $80 million more expensive, and requires maintaining the mainframe and new platform simultaneously during the transition (estimated $15 million/year additional operational cost). Additionally, the bank must maintain COBOL expertise for the 48-month transition period, and only 5 COBOL programmers remain on staff.

The CEO wants the faster, cheaper option. The CRO (Chief Risk Officer) wants the safer option. The regulator has asked to review whichever approach is selected.

### Question 5
Based on the scenario above, what factors should the Architecture Board consider, and which approach should they recommend?

a) Recommend the Strangler Fig Pattern (Project Evolution). The Architecture Board should consider: risk to business continuity (a failed big-bang cutover could be existential for a $50 billion bank), regulatory expectations (the regulator has already expressed concern about big-bang), the TOGAF principle that Transition Architectures should manage risk (not maximize it), and the 60% failure rate for big-bang banking migrations. The additional $80 million cost and 18 months are justified insurance against catastrophic failure. Address the COBOL expertise risk by contracting with specialized COBOL maintenance firms. Present the regulator with a detailed migration plan showing incremental risk management.

b) Recommend the Big-Bang Replacement (Project Phoenix) but with enhanced risk mitigation. The 30-month timeline and $200 million cost are significantly better. To address risk: invest $30 million in a comprehensive parallel-run environment that mirrors production for 6 months before cutover, establish a detailed fallback plan that can restore mainframe operations within 4 hours, and engage the regulator early to build confidence in the approach. The total cost ($230 million) is still less than the Strangler Fig option ($280 million + $15 million/year operational overhead). Present this to the CEO as the optimal risk-adjusted approach.

c) Recommend a hybrid approach: use the Strangler Fig Pattern for the highest-risk functions (payments, deposit accounts — representing 60% of transaction volume) while executing a big-bang migration for lower-risk functions (trade finance, treasury management). This Transition Architecture addresses the CEO's desire for speed (lower-risk functions migrate faster) and the CRO's risk concerns (high-risk functions migrate incrementally). Create two parallel workstreams with a unified integration architecture. Present the hybrid plan to the regulator as a risk-calibrated approach.

d) Recommend that the Architecture Board defer the decision and commission a detailed risk quantification analysis before choosing. Neither option has been subjected to rigorous risk-adjusted cost analysis. Engage an independent consulting firm to: quantify the probability and financial impact of big-bang failure, model the total cost of ownership for the 48-month Strangler Fig transition (including operational overhead and COBOL expertise costs), and assess regulatory approval probability for each approach. Present the risk-adjusted financial comparison to the Architecture Board within 8 weeks. This prevents a $200-280 million decision from being made on qualitative arguments.

## Scenario 6: Architecture Principles Conflicting with New Regulation

TrustVault Financial is a wealth management firm managing $200 billion in client assets across the United States and Europe. The company has a well-established set of architecture principles that have guided technology decisions for 5 years. One of the foundational principles is "Unified Client Data" which states: "All client data shall be maintained in a single, consolidated data store to ensure consistency, enable comprehensive risk analysis, and provide a unified client view across all business functions." This principle has driven the firm's investment of $45 million in a centralized Client Data Hub.

A new EU regulation, the "Digital Operational Resilience Act" (DORA), requires financial firms to demonstrate operational resilience, including the ability to continue operations if any single system fails. The firm's compliance team has concluded that DORA requires TrustVault to ensure that a failure of the centralized Client Data Hub does not bring all business operations to a halt. Additionally, DORA requires that critical business functions can be recovered within 2 hours of a major system failure.

The architecture team's analysis reveals a fundamental conflict: the "Unified Client Data" principle has created a single point of failure. If the Client Data Hub goes down, all 14 business applications that depend on it — trading, portfolio management, reporting, compliance, and client servicing — stop functioning. The current recovery time objective (RTO) for the Hub is 8 hours, well above DORA's 2-hour requirement. Reducing the RTO to 2 hours is technically feasible but would cost $25 million in redundancy infrastructure and ongoing operational costs of $5 million annually.

The alternative is to partially decentralize client data — allow critical business functions to maintain local data copies that can operate independently if the Hub fails. However, this directly contradicts the "Unified Client Data" principle and introduces data consistency challenges that the principle was designed to prevent. The CISO supports data decentralization for resilience but the Chief Data Officer opposes it, arguing that data consistency is more important than any regulation.

### Question 6
Based on the scenario above, how should the architecture team resolve the conflict between the architecture principle and the regulatory requirement?

a) Revise the "Unified Client Data" architecture principle to accommodate DORA requirements. Architecture principles are not immutable — TOGAF states that principles should be reviewed and updated when business context changes. The revised principle could read: "Client data shall be managed to ensure consistency as the primary goal, while maintaining operational resilience through controlled data replication for critical business functions. The Client Data Hub remains the system of record, with read replicas authorized for critical systems to meet regulatory resilience requirements." Develop a data architecture that implements CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation) — writes go to the Hub, reads can come from local replicas.

b) Maintain the "Unified Client Data" principle and invest the $25 million to achieve the 2-hour RTO through infrastructure redundancy (active-active Hub deployment across multiple data centers with automatic failover). The architecture principle exists for good reasons — data consistency is foundational to financial services. Breaking the principle to save money introduces data consistency risks that could lead to financial reporting errors, regulatory violations (different from DORA), and poor client outcomes. The $25 million investment is the cost of maintaining architectural integrity while meeting the new regulation.

c) Escalate the decision to the Architecture Board and present both options with a comprehensive trade-off analysis: Option A (principle revision with data decentralization) — lower cost, introduces data consistency complexity, DORA-compliant. Option B (principle preservation with infrastructure investment) — higher cost, maintains data consistency, DORA-compliant. Include risk assessments for both approaches. The Architecture Board, as the custodian of architecture principles, is the appropriate body to decide whether to revise a foundational principle. The architect should present the analysis, not make the decision.

d) Propose a time-bound compromise: immediately implement a "resilience overlay" that provides critical business functions with cached copies of essential client data sufficient for 2-hour continued operation (meeting DORA) while maintaining the "Unified Client Data" principle for normal operations. This is not a full decentralization — it's a disaster recovery mechanism. Simultaneously, launch an ADM iteration to properly redesign the data architecture to natively accommodate both resilience and consistency requirements. The overlay provides DORA compliance now while the architecture team develops the optimal long-term solution.

## Scenario 7: Architecture for Multi-Country Regulatory Diversity

GlobalPharma International is a pharmaceutical company operating in 20 countries with $40 billion in annual revenue. The company manufactures and distributes prescription medications, over-the-counter drugs, and medical devices. The CEO has commissioned an enterprise architecture initiative to create a unified digital platform for operations across all 20 countries.

The challenge is regulatory diversity: each country has different requirements for drug approval processes, pharmacovigilance reporting, supply chain tracking (serialization), clinical trial data management, pricing regulations, and data privacy. Some examples: the US FDA requires specific electronic submission formats, the EU EMA requires a different format, Japan's PMDA has unique requirements, and Brazil's ANVISA has recently changed its regulations entirely. Some countries require local data storage; others allow cross-border data flows with restrictions. Five countries have no digital regulatory framework at all, relying on paper-based processes.

The current state is highly fragmented: each country operation has its own IT systems, many of which are customized versions of the same base platforms. There are 14 different instances of SAP across the 20 countries, each heavily customized. The company spends $180 million annually on IT across all countries, and an estimated 40% ($72 million) is on maintaining country-specific customizations. Previous attempts to standardize have failed because country managers resist losing their customized systems, arguing that "our regulatory environment is unique."

The Architecture Board has asked the enterprise architect to propose a target architecture structure that addresses regulatory diversity while enabling operational efficiency. The $72 million in customization maintenance costs is the primary financial driver.

### Question 7
Based on the scenario above, how should the enterprise architect structure the architecture for multi-country regulatory diversity?

a) Design a "Global Platform, Local Compliance" architecture using TOGAF's Architecture Partitioning. Create a Strategic Architecture that defines a single global business process architecture, data architecture, and application platform. Define clear variation points where country-specific regulatory requirements create necessary divergence. Implement these variation points through a configurable compliance engine that adapts the global platform's behavior based on country-specific rule sets. Classify the global platform as a Common Systems Architecture in the Enterprise Continuum and country-specific configurations as Organization-Specific extensions.

b) Adopt a federated architecture with three tiers: Global (shared infrastructure, core data model, enterprise integration), Regional (US/Americas, EU/EMEA, Asia-Pacific — each region sharing regulatory similarities), and Country (country-specific regulatory compliance layers). Use TOGAF's Segment Architecture approach to define each tier independently. This allows countries within a region to share compliance capabilities (e.g., all EU countries can share EMA submission infrastructure) while accommodating country-specific needs at the lowest tier. Consolidate the 14 SAP instances to 3 regional instances with country-specific configuration layers.

c) Start by creating a comprehensive regulatory taxonomy that maps all 20 countries' requirements across all business functions. Use this taxonomy to identify true regulatory differences versus differences driven by organizational preference. Engage country managers to validate which customizations are genuinely regulatory and which are business preferences. Then design the target architecture with mandatory standardization for non-regulatory functions and supported variation only for validated regulatory requirements. This evidence-based approach reduces the "our regulations are unique" argument.

d) Recommend a phased approach starting with the 5 countries that have the most similar regulatory environments. Implement a standardized platform for these 5 countries as a proof of concept, demonstrating that regulatory compliance and standardization can coexist. Use the successful implementation to build the business case for broader rollout. Develop country-specific Transition Architectures for each subsequent group of countries, ordered by regulatory similarity. This reduces risk and builds organizational confidence in the standardization approach.

## Scenario 8: Rationalizing Service Sprawl

DigiCommerce Global is a $12 billion e-commerce company that adopted a microservices architecture 5 years ago. What began as a disciplined service-oriented architecture has devolved into service sprawl: the company now operates 534 microservices, many of which have overlapping functionality. There are 7 different services that manage some aspect of customer identity, 5 that handle pricing calculations, 4 that manage inventory state, and 12 that perform some form of order validation. The original architecture team that designed the initial service boundaries has largely left the company.

The consequences are severe: operational costs have increased by 60% due to the complexity of managing 534 services. The mean time to deploy a new feature has increased from 2 weeks to 8 weeks because changes typically require coordinating across 5-10 services. The platform experiences an average of 3 production incidents per day caused by inter-service communication failures. Debugging production issues takes an average of 4 hours because distributed tracing across hundreds of services is inadequate. The company's cloud infrastructure bill is $42 million annually, and an estimated 30% ($12.6 million) is wasted on underutilized services.

The CTO has asked the enterprise architecture team to develop an architecture rationalization plan. However, the 534 services are owned by 45 different development teams, each of whom has built their services with their own technology choices, data stores, and API standards. Team autonomy has been a core engineering culture value. The VP of Engineering has warned that any attempt to "centralize or standardize" will be met with resistance and could trigger talent attrition. The company plans an IPO in 18 months and cannot afford major operational disruptions or talent losses.

### Question 8
Based on the scenario above, how should the enterprise architecture team rationalize the service sprawl?

a) Conduct a comprehensive service portfolio analysis using TOGAF's architecture approach. Map all 534 services to business capabilities, identify redundancies, and produce a target Application Architecture that consolidates services to approximately 150 well-defined services aligned with bounded contexts from Domain-Driven Design. Create a 3-phase migration roadmap: Phase 1 (months 1-6) — consolidate the most duplicated services (identity, pricing, inventory, order validation); Phase 2 (months 7-12) — standardize APIs and shared infrastructure; Phase 3 (months 13-18) — optimize remaining services. Present this to the Architecture Board for approval and enforce through governance.

b) Avoid a top-down rationalization mandate. Instead, establish architectural guardrails and let teams self-organize the consolidation. Define: a service taxonomy (based on business capability mapping) that makes service ownership and overlap visible, shared API standards and an API gateway to manage inter-service communication, a shared observability platform for distributed tracing, and "service health scorecards" that measure each service's operational quality. Create an Architecture Review process for new services that prevents further sprawl. Use the Enterprise Continuum to define reference architectures for common service patterns. The transparency and standards will create natural consolidation pressure without mandating specific changes.

c) Prioritize the operational crisis first: focus on the 3 daily production incidents and the 8-week deployment time rather than architectural rationalization. Implement a shared service mesh for inter-service communication, deploy comprehensive distributed tracing, and establish a production reliability team. Once operational stability is achieved (target: 3-6 months), use the observability data to identify which services are truly problematic and focus rationalization efforts there. This approach delivers immediate business value (platform stability ahead of IPO) while building the data foundation for targeted rationalization.

d) Recommend a "domain-oriented" reorganization: group the 534 services into business domains (e.g., Customer, Order, Inventory, Payment, Fulfillment) and assign a domain architecture team to each. Each domain team maps their services to business capabilities, identifies internal redundancies, and proposes consolidation within their domain. Cross-domain redundancies (e.g., multiple services managing customer identity) are resolved through inter-domain architecture working groups. This federated approach respects team autonomy, distributes the analysis workload, and creates accountability for rationalization at the domain level.

## Answer Key

### Scenario 1 - Question 1
**Scoring:**
- **Answer b) = 5 points (Best):** This phased approach is the most effective TOGAF-aligned response because it addresses the immediate constraint (no commercial solution exists) while creating an architectural path toward the ultimate capability. Using Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs) to define the Adjudication Engine interface is precisely what TOGAF recommends — ABBs define "what" a component does without specifying "how," allowing the implementation to evolve. The Transition Architecture delivers business value immediately (sequential processing) while the architectural extension points enable the target capability to be added incrementally. The vendor platform selection in Phase D provides the foundation while the custom component addresses the differentiated capability. This balances cost, risk, and strategic value more effectively than any other option.
- **Answer a) = 3 points (Second):** Adjusting the target architecture to match available capability has TOGAF support — the ADM is iterative, and targets should be achievable. The concept of "design for extension" is architecturally sound. However, this approach accepts a permanently diminished Business Architecture (sequential processing vs. multi-jurisdiction automation) without exploring whether the capability gap can be partially closed. The Chief Actuary's warning that the 45-to-5-day target is unachievable without multi-jurisdiction automation suggests that the revised target architecture may not deliver the business value that justified the initiative.
- **Answer d) = 1 point (Third):** A 3-month pause for market study and proof of concept is prudent but risks analysis paralysis in a competitive market. The gap analysis has already been done, and the market assessment (no COTS solution exists) is clear. The proof of concept for AI/ML approach has merit but could have been commissioned as a parallel activity without pausing the entire initiative. This approach is too conservative for an organization that needs to transform its claims processing to remain competitive.
- **Answer c) = 0 points (Distractor):** While the Enterprise Continuum classification is technically correct (this would be Organization-Specific), supporting a $60 million, 3-year custom build without exploring phased or hybrid alternatives is reckless. The competitor's $120 million, 4-year experience suggests the CTO's estimate may be optimistic. A dedicated ADM cycle for the Adjudication Engine alone doesn't address the immediate need for a claims platform or the vendor platform selection that constrains future automation options. This approach puts all eggs in the custom-build basket without architectural risk management.

### Scenario 2 - Question 2
