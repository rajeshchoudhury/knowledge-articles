## Scenario 1: Setting Up an Architecture Capability

OrionEnergy is a large energy utility company that serves 4 million residential and 200,000 commercial customers across three states. The company has recently appointed a new Chief Digital Officer (CDO) who has identified that the organization's technology landscape is fragmented: there are over 300 applications (many redundant), no consistent approach to technology investment decisions, and individual business units make IT purchases independently with no central oversight.

The CDO has secured board approval and budget to establish a formal enterprise architecture capability. She has hired a seasoned enterprise architect, Marcus, to build this capability from the ground up. The company currently has no architecture governance, no documented principles, no repository, and no formal architecture roles. The IT department has 450 staff, and there are strong departmental silos. The heads of the Customer Service, Grid Operations, and Energy Trading divisions are protective of their technology autonomy and have expressed concern that "corporate architecture" will slow them down.

Marcus must define the approach for establishing the architecture capability.

### Question 1
What should Marcus recommend as the approach for establishing the enterprise architecture capability at OrionEnergy?

a) Focus the Preliminary Phase on establishing comprehensive governance first—define a complete set of architecture principles, create a full Architecture Repository structure, establish the Architecture Board with formal authority over all technology decisions, and mandate that no technology purchases can proceed without architecture approval  
b) Take an incremental approach to the Preliminary Phase: start by defining a minimal viable set of architecture principles co-created with division heads, establish a lightweight governance framework with an advisory (not mandating) Architecture Board initially, identify a high-value pilot domain to demonstrate architecture value, and plan to mature the capability iteratively as credibility is built  
c) Skip the Preliminary Phase and start directly with Phase A on the most visible technology problem (application rationalization of the 300+ applications) to demonstrate immediate value, and establish governance organically as the architecture practice proves its worth  
d) Commission a 6-month architecture maturity assessment using TOGAF's Architecture Capability Framework before doing any architecture work, to fully understand the current state and ensure the capability is built on a thorough understanding of organizational readiness

## Scenario 2: Choosing Between Architecture Approaches

QuantumPharma is a global pharmaceutical company that needs to modernize its drug development data management systems. The current systems were built 15 years ago and struggle with the volume and variety of data generated by modern clinical trials, genomics research, and real-world evidence studies. Two competing architecture approaches have been proposed:

**Approach A (Big Bang Replacement):** Replace all existing data management systems with a single, integrated, cloud-native data platform built on a modern data lakehouse architecture. This would take approximately 24 months, cost $45 million, and provide a state-of-the-art unified platform. During the transition, the existing systems would continue to operate with minimal investment. The VP of R&D strongly supports this approach, arguing that the current systems are fundamentally inadequate and incremental fixes are "putting lipstick on a pig."

**Approach B (Evolutionary Architecture):** Implement a data mesh approach that gradually modernizes capabilities domain by domain. Start with clinical trial data (highest pain point), then expand to genomics, then real-world evidence. Each domain would take 6-8 months. A thin integration layer would connect modernized domains with legacy systems during transition. Total estimated cost is $35 million over 30 months. The Chief Data Officer supports this approach, citing lower risk and earlier value delivery.

Both approaches align with the Target Architecture defined in the Architecture Vision. The enterprise architect must recommend one approach to the Architecture Board.

### Question 2
Which approach should the enterprise architect recommend, and what is the architectural justification?

a) Recommend Approach A (Big Bang Replacement) because it delivers the cleanest architecture with no compromise or technical debt, fully aligns with the target state in a single implementation, eliminates the need for temporary integration solutions, and achieves the target architecture faster despite the higher upfront cost  
b) Recommend Approach B (Evolutionary Architecture) because it uses Transition Architectures consistent with TOGAF's ADM, delivers business value incrementally starting with the highest-pain domain, reduces risk through phased delivery, allows learning from each phase to improve subsequent phases, and maintains business continuity through the integration layer—while ultimately reaching the same target architecture  
c) Recommend a hybrid approach: start with Approach B's clinical trial domain as the first Transition Architecture, but plan for a comprehensive migration of all remaining domains simultaneously as the second Transition Architecture, combining the quick-win of Approach B with the efficiency of Approach A  
d) Defer the decision and recommend conducting a six-month proof-of-concept for both approaches, building small prototypes of each to generate empirical data for the Architecture Board's decision

## Scenario 3: Handling Resistance to Enterprise Architecture

MetroBank is a mid-sized bank where the enterprise architecture practice was established two years ago. Despite initial executive support, the architecture practice has struggled to gain traction. A recent internal survey revealed significant resistance:

- 65% of project managers view architecture reviews as "bureaucratic gatekeeping" that delays their projects by 3-4 weeks
- The Head of Digital Banking publicly stated that "architecture is where innovation goes to die" and has been running projects without architecture engagement
- Development teams bypass architecture standards by using cloud services directly, citing the speed advantage
- The Architecture Board meetings have become poorly attended, with only 2 of 7 members regularly participating
- However, two major project failures in the past year were attributed (in post-mortem reviews) to lack of architectural oversight—a failed payment system integration and a data breach caused by inconsistent security standards

The Chief Architect has been asked by the CIO to "fix the architecture practice or we'll disband it."

### Question 3
What should the Chief Architect recommend to revitalize the architecture practice?

a) Strengthen governance enforcement: require the CIO to mandate architecture compliance for all projects, institute penalties for teams that bypass architecture reviews, and make architecture approval a formal gate in the project lifecycle that cannot be skipped  
b) Transform the architecture practice from a governance-focused function to a value-enabling service: streamline the review process to under one week, embed architects within project teams as advisors rather than gatekeepers, use the two project failures as case studies to demonstrate architecture's risk-reduction value, create a self-service architecture guidance portal, and re-engage the Architecture Board with a refreshed charter focused on enabling innovation while managing risk  
c) Disband the current architecture practice and replace it with a federated model where each business unit has its own architect reporting to the business unit head, with a lightweight coordination function to ensure minimal alignment on security and integration standards  
d) Conduct a formal Architecture Capability Maturity assessment, present the results to the CIO and board with a 12-month improvement plan, and request additional budget and headcount to address the identified capability gaps before attempting any changes

## Scenario 4: SOA Architecture Decision

InsureAll is a national insurance company that has been operating with a monolithic claims processing system for 12 years. The system handles auto, home, and life insurance claims, processing 50,000 claims daily. The company wants to modernize using a service-oriented architecture (SOA) approach to improve agility and enable new digital channels.

During Phase C (Application Architecture), the architect identifies the following situation:
- The monolithic system has deeply intertwined business logic, with auto, home, and life insurance sharing common code for fraud detection, payment processing, and customer notification
- A microservices proof-of-concept for the auto claims module showed that decomposing the shared logic caused significant performance degradation (response times went from 200ms to 2 seconds) due to network calls between services
- The company needs to launch a new mobile claims submission app within 8 months
- The fraud detection algorithm is proprietary and considered a major competitive advantage—the security team is uncomfortable distributing it across multiple services
- The existing development team (60 people) has no experience with microservices, distributed tracing, or container orchestration

### Question 4
What SOA/service architecture approach should the architect recommend?

a) Proceed with a full microservices decomposition as originally planned, accepting the performance degradation as a temporary cost of modernization that will be resolved through infrastructure optimization, caching, and eventual team upskilling  
b) Adopt a pragmatic, bounded service approach: decompose the monolith into a small number of coarse-grained domain services (auto claims, home claims, life claims) with shared services for cross-cutting concerns (fraud detection, payments, notifications), maintain the fraud detection algorithm as a single secure service with controlled access, expose a new API layer for the mobile app while keeping core processing intact, and plan for further decomposition as the team matures  
c) Abandon the SOA approach entirely and instead invest in modernizing the monolithic architecture with a new technology stack, API wrapper, and improved deployment pipeline, since the monolith's performance and the team's skills don't support service decomposition  
d) Outsource the modernization to a consulting firm with microservices expertise, having them build the new architecture while the internal team continues maintaining the monolith, and then transition the internal team to the new architecture once it's built

## Scenario 5: Security Architecture Decision

GovCloud Agency is a government agency that is migrating its citizen services portal to a cloud environment. The portal handles sensitive citizen data including tax records, social security information, and healthcare enrollment. During Phase D (Technology Architecture), a critical security architecture decision must be made.

The agency's current security architecture uses a traditional perimeter-based model with firewalls, VPNs, and a DMZ. The security team has proposed three approaches for the cloud environment:

- **Option 1:** Replicate the current perimeter-based security model in the cloud using virtual firewalls and VPN connections, maintaining the same security controls the agency has used for 10 years
- **Option 2:** Implement a Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) that verifies every access request regardless of network location, uses microsegmentation, continuous authentication, and least-privilege access
- **Option 3:** Use the cloud provider's native security services exclusively, relying on their built-in security controls and compliance certifications

The agency must comply with FedRAMP requirements, and a recent Inspector General audit cited the agency for "insufficient identity management controls" in the current environment. The cloud migration must be completed within 18 months, and the security team has 12 members, only 3 of whom have cloud security experience.

### Question 5
What security architecture approach should the architect recommend?

a) Recommend Option 1 (perimeter-based) because the team already understands it, it provides continuity of security controls, it can be implemented fastest since the team has 10 years of experience with this model, and it avoids the risk of introducing unfamiliar security paradigms during a major migration  
b) Recommend Option 2 (Zero Trust Architecture) as the target security architecture, but implement it incrementally through Transition Architectures: start with identity and access management modernization (addressing the IG audit finding), then implement microsegmentation for the most sensitive data (tax, SSN), then expand ZTA to all services—while providing targeted training to upskill the security team at each stage and using cloud-native security services where they align with ZTA principles  
c) Recommend Option 3 (cloud-native security) as the simplest path to FedRAMP compliance since the cloud provider already has FedRAMP authorization, and supplement it with additional identity management controls to address the IG audit finding  
d) Recommend implementing all three options in layers: perimeter-based as the outer layer, cloud-native services as the middle layer, and Zero Trust as the inner layer for the most sensitive data, creating a defense-in-depth strategy that leverages all approaches

## Scenario 6: Handling Non-Compliance Findings

Atlas Telecommunications is a large telco with a mature enterprise architecture practice. During a scheduled Architecture Compliance review of the company's new 5G network management platform (which is 60% complete and scheduled for launch in 5 months), the review team discovers multiple compliance issues:

**Critical Finding:** The platform uses an unapproved open-source real-time database for network performance monitoring instead of the enterprise-standard time-series database. The project team claims the enterprise standard cannot handle the data volumes generated by 5G network monitoring (10 million events per second).

**Major Finding:** The platform's API gateway was custom-built instead of using the enterprise API management platform. The team states that the enterprise platform's latency (50ms per call) is unacceptable for real-time network management where sub-10ms response times are required.

**Minor Finding:** The platform's monitoring dashboard uses a different visualization framework than the enterprise standard, creating an inconsistent user experience for network operations staff.

The 5G platform is strategically critical—the company's competitive position depends on being among the first to offer comprehensive 5G management to enterprise customers. The CEO has publicly committed to the launch date.

### Question 6
How should the Architecture Board handle these non-compliance findings?

a) Issue a blanket dispensation for all three findings given the strategic importance and CEO commitment, require the project team to document all deviations, and schedule a post-launch review to assess whether the deviations should become new standards  
b) Address each finding proportionally: for the critical finding, require the project team to provide a documented technical justification with performance benchmarks proving the enterprise standard cannot meet requirements, and if validated, initiate a standards review process to evaluate the open-source database for enterprise adoption; for the major finding, similarly require performance evidence and consider updating the API management standard; for the minor finding, grant a dispensation with a requirement to migrate to the enterprise visualization standard within 12 months post-launch—and in all cases, ensure the findings are fed back into the architecture standards review process  
c) Require the project team to halt development and switch to enterprise standards for all three findings, since allowing non-compliance on a high-profile project will encourage other teams to bypass standards, and the enterprise standards exist for good reasons including security, supportability, and consistency  
d) Escalate all findings to the CTO and CEO for a business decision, since the architecture standards conflict with the strategic project requirements and this is fundamentally a business trade-off that the Architecture Board should not make unilaterally

## Scenario 7: Architecture Board Decision on Competing Initiatives

Pinnacle Retail Group operates 200 department stores and a growing e-commerce platform. The Architecture Board faces a critical decision in its quarterly meeting. Two major initiatives have been proposed, but budget constraints mean only one can proceed this fiscal year:

**Initiative A: Unified Commerce Platform**
Proposed by the VP of Digital Commerce. Would replace the separate in-store POS system and e-commerce platform with a unified commerce architecture enabling buy-online-pickup-in-store (BOPIS), ship-from-store, and seamless inventory visibility. Estimated cost: $18 million over 18 months. Business case: $25 million annual revenue increase from improved customer experience, 15% reduction in lost sales due to inventory visibility.

**Initiative B: Supply Chain Intelligence Platform**
Proposed by the VP of Supply Chain. Would implement AI-driven demand forecasting, automated supplier management, and real-time supply chain visibility. Estimated cost: $12 million over 12 months. Business case: $20 million annual cost savings from inventory optimization, 30% reduction in stockouts, 20% reduction in excess inventory.

Both initiatives align with the enterprise architecture strategy. The current enterprise architecture supports either initiative but implementing both simultaneously would require architectural changes to the shared data layer that neither initiative's budget covers. The Architecture Board has 7 members, and opinions are split.

### Question 7
What should the Architecture Board recommend?

a) Recommend Initiative A (Unified Commerce) because it directly impacts customer experience and revenue, is more transformational and strategic, and positions the company for the future of retail—even though it costs more and takes longer  
b) Recommend Initiative B (Supply Chain) be implemented first because it has lower cost, shorter timeline, higher ROI, and its inventory optimization outputs are a prerequisite for Initiative A's inventory visibility features—then sequence Initiative A as the following initiative, ensuring the shared data layer architecture is designed during Initiative B to accommodate both initiatives, and include the shared data layer investment in Initiative B's scope  
c) Recommend implementing both initiatives simultaneously with reduced scope—prioritize the highest-value features from each initiative and implement them as a single combined program, sharing the development team and data layer investment across both  
d) Recommend deferring both initiatives for one quarter while the architecture team conducts a detailed technical feasibility study and develops a comprehensive business case comparison, ensuring the Architecture Board has complete information for a data-driven decision

## Scenario 8: Choosing Appropriate Deliverables

NovaTech Solutions is a technology company that has just completed Phase A (Architecture Vision) for a major cloud migration initiative. The company is moving from a traditional on-premises data center to a multi-cloud architecture across AWS and Azure. The architecture team, consisting of a lead architect and two junior architects, is about to begin Phases B through D.

The lead architect is debating the appropriate level of documentation and deliverables. The project has the following characteristics:
- Timeline: 10 months to complete Phases B through D and begin Phase E
- Stakeholders: 6 key stakeholders ranging from the CFO (concerned about cloud costs) to the VP of Engineering (concerned about developer experience) to the CISO (concerned about security posture)
- Complexity: 85 applications to be assessed for cloud migration (rehost, replatform, refactor, retain, or retire)
- Risk: Two previous cloud migration attempts failed due to incomplete planning
- The company culture is agile and documentation-averse; teams prefer working software over comprehensive documentation

The junior architects want to create full TOGAF deliverables for every artifact defined in the Content Framework. The lead architect is concerned this will consume the entire timeline and produce documents that no one reads.

### Question 8
What approach to deliverables should the lead architect recommend?

a) Create the full set of TOGAF Content Framework deliverables as the junior architects suggest, because previous migrations failed due to incomplete planning and comprehensive documentation is the best way to prevent a third failure  
b) Tailor the deliverable set based on stakeholder concerns, project risks, and the organization's culture: create an Architecture Definition Document focused on the cloud target state and migration assessment for all 85 applications, produce specific views addressing each stakeholder's key concern (cost models for CFO, developer toolchain architecture for VP Engineering, security architecture for CISO), use lightweight catalog and matrix formats for application assessment, prioritize diagrams over text for the documentation-averse culture, and explicitly skip artifacts that don't address identified risks or stakeholder concerns  
c) Minimize documentation to a single Architecture Decision Record (ADR) log and an application migration spreadsheet, since the company culture is agile and the team should spend time on architecture work rather than documents, relying on verbal communication and whiteboard sessions for stakeholder engagement  
d) Adopt the junior architects' comprehensive approach for Phases B and C, then evaluate whether the same level of detail is needed for Phase D, adjusting the deliverable scope based on lessons learned from the first two domain architectures

### Key Themes Across All Scenarios
1. **Pragmatism over dogmatism** — TOGAF is designed to be tailored, not rigidly applied
2. **Proportional governance** — Responses should match the severity and context of issues
3. **Transition Architectures** — Incremental approaches almost always outperform big-bang approaches
4. **Stakeholder awareness** — Technical decisions must account for organizational reality
5. **Evidence-based decisions** — Require justification for deviations rather than blanket acceptance or rejection
6. **Architecture as enabler** — Architecture should enable business value, not just control technology decisions
7. **Iterative maturity** — Architecture practices and capabilities mature over time, not overnight
8. **Risk-aware planning** — Dependencies, constraints, and trade-offs must be explicitly managed
