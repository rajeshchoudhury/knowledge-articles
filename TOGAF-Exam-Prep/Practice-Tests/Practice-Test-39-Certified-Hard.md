## Scenario 1: Disruptive Technology Invalidating a 3-Year Roadmap

Pinnacle Manufacturing is a $15 billion industrial automation company that began its enterprise architecture-guided digital transformation 3 years ago. The Architecture Board approved a comprehensive architecture roadmap that includes five Transition Architectures over 5 years. Transition Architectures 1 and 2 have been successfully implemented: the company migrated to cloud infrastructure and deployed a modern ERP system. Transition Architecture 3 is underway — implementing an IoT platform for predictive maintenance across 200 factories.

The industry has been fundamentally disrupted by generative AI and autonomous manufacturing systems. Three of Pinnacle's major competitors have announced "lights-out factories" powered by AI agents that can manage entire production lines without human intervention. Industry analysts predict that companies without autonomous manufacturing capabilities will lose 30% market share within 5 years. The remaining Transition Architectures 4 (workforce collaboration platform) and 5 (supply chain optimization) are now strategically questionable — they assume human-centric manufacturing processes that may become obsolete.

The Chief Architect faces a critical decision. The current Phase H (Architecture Change Management) process includes monitoring of technology and business environment changes, and the AI disruption clearly qualifies as a significant change driver. However, the CEO has already committed $400 million to the existing roadmap (of which $250 million has been spent), and the board of directors approved the program based on the original 5-year business case. Transition Architecture 3 (IoT platform, $80 million) is 60% complete. The CTO argues for immediately pivoting to an AI-first architecture. The COO warns that disrupting TA3 will leave factories without the promised predictive maintenance capability. The CFO insists the existing budget commitment must be honored.

### Question 1
Based on the scenario above, should the Chief Architect trigger a full Phase H review or recommend restarting the ADM?

a) Trigger the Phase H Architecture Change Management process to formally assess the AI disruption as a technology change driver. Conduct an impact analysis on the existing architecture roadmap, evaluating which elements of TA3, TA4, and TA5 remain valid and which need revision. Based on the analysis, recommend modifications to the remaining Transition Architectures that incorporate AI/autonomous manufacturing capabilities while preserving the value of completed investments (TA1 and TA2). Present the findings to the Architecture Board with options ranging from minor roadmap adjustments to major architectural revision, with cost-benefit analysis for each option.

b) Recommend restarting the ADM from Phase A with a new Architecture Vision centered on autonomous manufacturing. The existing target architecture was designed for a pre-AI world and is now fundamentally misaligned with market reality. While TA1 (cloud infrastructure) and TA2 (modern ERP) remain valid as foundational capabilities, TA3-TA5 need to be reconceived entirely. A new ADM cycle allows the architecture team to properly assess the AI landscape, define new architecture principles, and create a target architecture that positions Pinnacle for the autonomous manufacturing era. The IoT platform (TA3) work should be paused pending the new Architecture Vision.

c) Continue TA3 to completion as planned while simultaneously launching a parallel ADM cycle from Phase A focused specifically on AI and autonomous manufacturing capabilities. TA3's IoT platform provides sensor data infrastructure that will be valuable regardless of whether manufacturing is human-centric or autonomous — IoT data feeds AI models. Complete TA3 to protect the $48 million already invested and deliver the promised predictive maintenance capability. The parallel ADM cycle can incorporate TA3's outputs as baseline when determining how AI capabilities should be layered onto the existing architecture. Defer decisions on TA4 and TA5 until the AI-focused Architecture Vision is complete.

d) Escalate to the board of directors with a recommendation to fundamentally restructure the program. Present an analysis showing that the remaining $150 million budget should be redirected from the current roadmap to an AI-first transformation. Engage an external consulting firm specializing in AI-driven manufacturing to assess Pinnacle's readiness and develop a target architecture. The enterprise architecture team should lead the governance of this new direction but acknowledge that the AI domain requires specialized expertise they may not possess. Cancel TA4 and TA5 and convert TA3 into an "AI Data Foundation" initiative.

## Scenario 2: Hidden Vendor Bias from the Project Sponsor

DataFlow Analytics is a $3 billion data analytics company that provides enterprise analytics platforms to Fortune 500 clients. The company has launched an enterprise architecture initiative to modernize its internal technology platform. The enterprise architect, James Chen, has been assigned to lead the ADM cycle for the platform modernization, which will replace the company's aging analytics infrastructure. The project sponsor is the SVP of Product, Lisa Park, who controls a $50 million modernization budget.

During Phase B (Business Architecture), James discovers that Lisa has already signed a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with CloudAnalytica, a specific cloud analytics vendor, committing to a $20 million license agreement contingent on the modernization project selecting their platform. The MOU was signed before the ADM cycle began and was not disclosed to the Architecture Board or the architecture team. James learns about it accidentally when a CloudAnalytica sales representative references the MOU in a conversation.

Investigating further, James discovers that Lisa was a board advisor to CloudAnalytica before joining DataFlow (a fact disclosed in her hiring but not connected to this initiative), and that CloudAnalytica's platform, while competent, scored third out of four vendors in a preliminary technical assessment conducted by the architecture team during environment scanning. Two other platforms — from vendors with no relationship to Lisa — scored higher on technical capability, scalability, and total cost of ownership.

James faces a dilemma: Lisa is his project sponsor and controls the budget. Reporting the conflict of interest could be career-threatening but ignoring it could result in a suboptimal technology decision and a governance violation. The Architecture Board has a principle of "vendor-neutral technology selection based on architecture requirements," and the governance framework requires that all technology decisions go through the Architecture Board's review process.

### Question 2
Based on the scenario above, how should James maintain architecture integrity?

a) Document the findings in a formal Architecture Decision Record: the preliminary vendor assessment results, the existence of the MOU, and Lisa's prior relationship with CloudAnalytica. Present this documentation to the Architecture Board Chair privately before the next board meeting. Recommend that the Architecture Board invoke its governance authority to require a formal, transparent vendor evaluation as part of Phase D (Technology Architecture), with evaluation criteria aligned to the approved architecture requirements. The Architecture Board should ensure that all vendors are evaluated on equal terms, regardless of any pre-existing commitments. Let the governance process handle the conflict of interest transparently.

b) Confront Lisa directly and professionally. Present the preliminary vendor assessment showing CloudAnalytica ranked third. Ask her to explain the MOU and how it aligns with the architecture governance principle of vendor-neutral selection. Give her the opportunity to disclose the MOU to the Architecture Board herself and potentially recuse herself from the vendor selection decision. If she refuses, escalate to the Architecture Board. This approach respects the sponsor relationship while giving Lisa a chance to self-correct before the governance process is invoked.

c) Continue the ADM cycle without raising the MOU issue. Ensure that Phase D produces a rigorous, well-documented technology architecture with clear evaluation criteria derived from the business and information systems architectures. When the vendor evaluation is formally conducted in Phase D, the technical evidence will objectively determine the best platform. If CloudAnalytica scores highest on merit, the MOU is irrelevant. If another vendor scores higher, the documented evaluation provides the Architecture Board with evidence to override the MOU. This approach lets the process work without creating a political confrontation.

d) Escalate immediately to the CIO or CEO, bypassing the Architecture Board. The MOU represents a potential conflict of interest and a governance violation that goes beyond architecture governance — it's an ethical and potentially legal issue. The architecture team is not the appropriate body to handle conflicts of interest involving senior executives. The CIO or CEO should determine whether the MOU violates company procurement policies and whether Lisa should be recused from the modernization initiative entirely.

## Scenario 3: Legacy System Decommissioning Without Retraining Budget

MetroTransit Authority is a public transportation agency serving 4 million daily riders across a major metropolitan area. The enterprise architecture team has completed a full ADM cycle and the target architecture includes replacing the 25-year-old "RouteMaster" system — a mainframe-based application that manages route planning, driver scheduling, and real-time dispatch operations. The replacement is a modern cloud-based platform called "TransitCloud."

The migration plan in Phase F identifies a critical constraint: 215 dispatchers, route planners, and operations supervisors depend on RouteMaster daily. These are experienced employees with an average tenure of 18 years who know RouteMaster's interface intimately. They use keyboard shortcuts, custom screen configurations, and undocumented workarounds that have evolved over decades. The migration plan calls for decommissioning RouteMaster 6 months after TransitCloud goes live.

The problem: the training budget for the entire migration was cut in the last round of budget reductions. The project has $0 allocated for user retraining. The project manager has proposed that users "learn by doing" with just a user manual and help desk support. The union representing dispatchers has objected, stating that inadequate training on a safety-critical system (dispatch manages real-time vehicle movements) creates unsafe working conditions. The transit authority's board is unwilling to restore the training budget because they believe the savings from decommissioning the mainframe ($3.2 million annually) should not be consumed by training costs. The Architecture Board must recommend an approach that enables the migration without dedicated training funding.

### Question 3
Based on the scenario above, what approach should the enterprise architect recommend?

a) Revise the Transition Architecture to include a longer parallel-operation period. Instead of decommissioning RouteMaster after 6 months, maintain it for 18 months while users gradually migrate to TransitCloud at their own pace. During the parallel period, make TransitCloud available alongside RouteMaster and create incentives for voluntary adoption (e.g., only TransitCloud gets new features). This "shadow deployment" approach allows organic learning without formal training. The additional 12 months of mainframe operation costs ($3.2 million) is the implicit "training budget" — it costs the same as formal training would but spreads the cost over time and avoids political conflict with the board.

b) Recommend that the Architecture Board flag this as an architecture risk that threatens the migration's success and escalate to the transit authority's board. Present a risk analysis: deploying a safety-critical system without training creates regulatory, safety, and operational risks that exceed the training cost. The enterprise architect has a responsibility to identify risks that affect architecture implementation — inadequate change management for a safety-critical system is exactly such a risk. Recommend that the board either restore training funding or accept a delayed migration timeline. Document this risk formally in the Architecture Risk Assessment.

c) Design the TransitCloud user interface to minimize the training requirement. Engage the 215 users in a user experience design process during Phase G that creates a TransitCloud interface mimicking RouteMaster's key workflows, keyboard shortcuts, and screen layouts. Implement "guided workflows" that walk users through new processes step by step. Create a "RouteMaster mode" skin that presents TransitCloud in a familiar interface. This approach addresses the root cause — the users' unfamiliarity with the new system — through architecture and design rather than training budget.

d) Implement a phased user migration plan that uses a "train-the-trainer" model requiring no external training budget. Identify 15-20 power users among the 215 staff who are technologically confident. Give them early access to TransitCloud during the pilot phase and support them with intensive help desk resources. These power users then become informal trainers for their peers, spreading knowledge through natural work relationships. Roll out TransitCloud in phases by route/dispatch zone, with each phase using the previous phase's users as mentors for the next. This leverages internal knowledge transfer at zero additional cost.

## Scenario 4: Inquorate Architecture Board Undermining Governance

Continental Financial Group is a diversified financial services company with banking, insurance, and asset management divisions. The Architecture Board was established 3 years ago with 12 members: the CIO (chair), the Chief Architect, division CIOs from the three business units, the CISO, the Chief Data Officer, VP of Infrastructure, VP of Applications, the PMO Director, the Head of Compliance, and the Head of Innovation. The board was designed to provide comprehensive governance across all architectural decisions.

Over the past 18 months, attendance has steadily declined. The last 6 meetings have had the following attendance: 5, 4, 6, 3, 5, 4 members (out of 12). The quorum for decision-making was set at 7 members when the board was established. This means no Architecture Board meeting in the past 18 months has been quorate — none of the decisions made during these meetings are formally valid.

During this period, the Architecture Board "approved" 14 architecture decisions, including: the enterprise cloud strategy, a data mesh architecture, selection of a major integration platform, 3 architecture dispensations, and the Architecture Vision for a $120 million digital transformation. These decisions guided real implementation work — $85 million in projects are currently underway based on these "approvals." The 3 members who consistently attend are the CIO, the Chief Architect, and the VP of Infrastructure. The other 9 members rotate sporadically, citing "meeting fatigue" and "too many governance committees."

The Head of Compliance has just noticed the quorum issue during a governance review and has raised it formally. She warns that the insurance regulator could view the inquorate decisions as a governance failure. The three division CIOs — whose sporadic attendance is the primary cause — argue that they "delegate their authority" to their architecture representatives who attend in their place, but no formal delegation protocol exists. The $85 million in ongoing projects cannot be stopped.

### Question 4
Based on the scenario above, how should the Chief Architect fix the governance situation?

a) Conduct a comprehensive Architecture Board restructuring. Reduce the board from 12 to 7 members — only those whose attendance is essential and achievable: CIO (chair), Chief Architect, CISO, Chief Data Officer, and one rotating division CIO representative. Set the quorum at 5 of 7. Create a formal delegation protocol that allows division CIOs to designate permanent alternates with full voting authority. Retroactively validate the 14 previous decisions by having the restructured board conduct a formal ratification review. Present this to the compliance team as a governance improvement program.

b) Immediately convene a special session of the full Architecture Board (all 12 members) to address the governance crisis. At this session: ratify all 14 previous decisions through a formal vote (validating the ongoing project work), establish a formal delegation and proxy voting protocol, implement a tiered governance model where only high-impact decisions require full board review while lower-impact decisions can be made by a smaller standing committee, and set consequences for persistent non-attendance. Do not restructure the board membership — instead, fix the engagement problem through better meeting management and reduced decision load.

c) Adopt a tiered governance model that aligns decision authority with decision significance. Create three tiers: Tier 1 (Strategic) — enterprise architecture vision, major technology strategy, cross-division standards — requires full Architecture Board (quorum of 7/12). Tier 2 (Significant) — segment architectures, major vendor selections, dispensations — requires a standing Architecture Review Committee of 5 senior members (quorum of 3/5). Tier 3 (Tactical) — project architecture reviews, minor standard exceptions — delegated to the Chief Architect with documentation. Retroactively classify and ratify the 14 decisions at the appropriate tier. Reform the full board to meet quarterly instead of monthly.

d) Accept that the 12-member Architecture Board model has failed and replace it with a "Rolling Architecture Governance" approach. Instead of periodic board meetings, architecture decisions are circulated to relevant stakeholders asynchronously through a digital governance platform. Each decision has a defined review period (72 hours for tactical, 2 weeks for strategic), a defined set of required reviewers based on the decision's scope, and an automatic approval if the required reviewers don't respond within the review period. This eliminates the meeting attendance problem entirely and creates a documented decision trail for regulatory compliance.

## Scenario 5: "Consistent" but Not "Conformant" at 80% Completion

StellarTech Aerospace is a defense contractor developing advanced avionics systems. The company is executing a program to modernize its engineering data management platform under an approved target architecture. During a Phase G architecture compliance review, Project Orion — the most critical implementation project ($65 million, 24-month timeline) — has been rated "Consistent" but not "Conformant" with the target architecture.

The assessment found that Project Orion's architecture is aligned with the spirit and direction of the target architecture — it uses the approved technology stack, follows the mandated integration patterns, and adheres to the security architecture. However, it deviates in three specific areas: (1) the data model uses a proprietary extension to the approved data standard instead of the standard's official extension mechanism, resulting in data that cannot be natively consumed by other systems without a translation layer; (2) the user interface was built using a framework that is not on the approved technology standards list (though it is technically superior to the approved option); and (3) the deployment architecture uses a single-region cloud deployment instead of the mandated multi-region deployment for disaster recovery.

Project Orion is 80% complete with 5 months remaining. The program manager argues that: deviation (1) would cost $2 million and 3 months to fix, deviation (2) is a better technical choice that should update the standard rather than the project, and deviation (3) can be addressed post-launch through a separate infrastructure project. The project delivers to the Department of Defense, which requires compliance with approved architectures as a contractual obligation. The Architecture Board must decide how to handle this assessment.

### Question 5
Based on the scenario above, what action should the Architecture Board take?

a) Require Project Orion to remediate all three deviations before project completion. The "Consistent" rating means the project is architecturally aligned but not compliant — and the DoD contractual obligation makes compliance non-negotiable. Deviation (1) affects data interoperability, a core architecture requirement. Deviation (2) violates approved standards, regardless of technical merit. Deviation (3) compromises disaster recovery, a critical capability for defense systems. The Architecture Board should approve a timeline extension and additional budget to achieve full conformance. Recommend updating the standard to include the superior UI framework, but only after proper evaluation.

b) Apply differentiated governance responses based on each deviation's impact and remediation cost. For deviation (1) — data model proprietary extension — require immediate remediation because data interoperability is a core architecture requirement and a likely DoD compliance point. For deviation (2) — UI framework — initiate a formal architecture change request to evaluate adding the framework to the approved standards list; grant a temporary dispensation pending the evaluation outcome. For deviation (3) — single-region deployment — grant a dispensation with a committed remediation plan to implement multi-region within 6 months post-launch, since this can be addressed at the infrastructure level without impacting the application. Document all decisions for DoD compliance reporting.

c) Accept the "Consistent" rating as sufficient for 80% project completion and defer conformance remediation to a post-launch phase. The project is architecturally aligned with the target architecture's intent, and the three deviations are manageable. Forcing remediation at 80% completion risks the project's timeline and budget without proportionate benefit. Create a formal post-launch Architecture Compliance Remediation Plan with specific milestones for addressing all three deviations within 12 months of launch. Present this plan to the DoD as evidence of governance and continuous improvement.

d) Escalate the compliance assessment to the DoD program office for their input before making a governance decision. Since the DoD contractually requires compliance with approved architectures, they are a critical stakeholder in this decision. The DoD may have their own view of which deviations are acceptable and which are not. The Architecture Board should present the three deviations with impact assessments and remediation costs, and ask the DoD to prioritize which deviations must be remediated before delivery versus which can be addressed post-delivery. This ensures the governance decision aligns with the customer's requirements.

## Scenario 6: Conflicting Data Models in Segment Architectures

MegaCorp Industries is a $45 billion conglomerate with four major business units: Aerospace, Automotive, Energy, and Healthcare. Each business unit developed its own Segment Architecture independently over the past 2 years, following the TOGAF ADM. The Segment Architectures were approved by the Architecture Board at different times and have been successfully guiding implementation within their respective business units.

A problem has emerged now that cross-business-unit initiatives are being planned. The four Segment Architectures have fundamentally conflicting data models for shared entities. "Customer" is defined differently across all four: Aerospace treats customers as government agencies and defense contractors; Automotive treats customers as OEMs and dealerships; Energy treats customers as utilities and industrial consumers; Healthcare treats customers as hospitals and insurance providers. Each data model captures different attributes, uses different identifiers, and implements different data quality rules.

The conflict becomes critical because the CEO's new strategic initiative — "One MegaCorp" — requires cross-selling across business units, unified customer analytics, and a consolidated CRM. The current data model conflicts mean that a large automotive OEM (Automotive customer) that also operates a hospital chain (Healthcare customer) and has a corporate energy contract (Energy customer) appears as three unrelated entities in the enterprise data landscape. There are an estimated 2,500 such cross-business-unit relationships.

The Strategic Architecture, approved 3 years ago, defined high-level data architecture principles ("enterprise data is an asset," "data should be shared across boundaries") but did not define a detailed enterprise data model — this was delegated to the Segment Architectures. The Chief Data Officer argues that a canonical enterprise data model is now needed. Each business unit's data architecture team argues that their model correctly represents their domain and shouldn't change. The Architecture Board must determine how to reconcile the conflicting data models within the Strategic Architecture framework.

### Question 6
Based on the scenario above, how should the conflicting data models be reconciled within the Strategic Architecture?

a) Develop a canonical enterprise data model that defines shared entities (Customer, Product, Contract, Location) at the enterprise level. Each business unit's Segment Architecture must conform its data model to the enterprise canonical model for shared entities while retaining domain-specific extensions for entities unique to their business. Use TOGAF's Data Architecture techniques to create the canonical model through cross-business-unit workshops. The "One MegaCorp" initiative should be the driver for an ADM iteration focused specifically on Data Architecture (Phase C) at the Strategic Architecture level. Implement a Master Data Management (MDM) platform as the technology solution.

b) Maintain each business unit's data model as-is but create an enterprise data integration layer that maps between the four models. Implement a "data virtualization" architecture that presents a unified view of cross-business-unit entities without requiring any Segment Architecture to change its data model. Use a golden record approach for the 2,500 cross-business-unit relationships, creating a federated identity resolution service. This preserves the integrity of each Segment Architecture while enabling the "One MegaCorp" vision through integration rather than standardization.

c) Revisit the Strategic Architecture to add the missing enterprise data model that should have been defined before the Segment Architectures were developed. Launch an ADM iteration at the Strategic Architecture level, starting at Phase C (Data Architecture), to define enterprise data standards, a canonical data model for shared entities, and data governance policies. Use this Strategic Architecture update to provide the overarching framework that the Segment Architectures should conform to. Each business unit then updates their Segment Architecture's data model to align with the new enterprise standard in their next ADM cycle. Accept that this is a multi-year convergence effort.

d) Create a pragmatic, phased approach: first, solve the immediate "One MegaCorp" need by building a Customer Data Platform (CDP) that aggregates customer data from all four business units using entity resolution techniques, without requiring any data model changes. This delivers the cross-selling and analytics capabilities within 6-12 months. Second, use the CDP implementation experience to inform the design of an enterprise data architecture standard. Third, in the next cycle of Segment Architecture updates, align data models to the enterprise standard where practical. This approach delivers business value first and standardizes second.

## Scenario 7: Technology Standards Dispensation Request

NovaTech Solutions is a technology services company with 15,000 employees delivering IT solutions to enterprise clients. The company's Architecture Board maintains a Technology Standards catalog that is updated annually and governs all technology selections for both internal systems and client-facing platforms. The current standards list was approved 8 months ago and includes specific approved technologies for each layer of the architecture stack.

The Digital Innovation team — a newly formed group reporting to the Chief Innovation Officer — has submitted a dispensation request to use a cloud-native application platform (a relatively new PaaS offering from a major cloud provider) for a client-facing product that is central to the company's growth strategy. This platform is not on the approved standards list. The Innovation team argues that the approved application platforms are too heavy for the rapid iteration and experimentation their product development process requires. They have data showing that development velocity on the cloud-native platform is 3x faster than on the approved alternatives.

The Architecture Board's review reveals complexity. The cloud-native platform was released 14 months ago and has seen rapid adoption in the industry (300,000 active organizations), but it doesn't yet have the enterprise support SLAs that NovaTech requires. The platform's data residency options don't cover 3 of the 20 countries where NovaTech operates. The platform uses a proprietary runtime that creates vendor lock-in — migrating away would require significant re-engineering. However, the competing approved platforms have been losing market share, and two industry analysts predict the cloud-native platform will become the industry standard within 3 years.

The CIO supports the Innovation team. The CISO is concerned about the missing enterprise SLAs and data residency gaps. The VP of Operations worries about supporting yet another platform. The Innovation team's product launch deadline is in 4 months, and switching to an approved platform now would delay the launch by 6 months.

### Question 7
Based on the scenario above, how should the Architecture Board evaluate the dispensation request?

a) Deny the dispensation request. The Technology Standards catalog exists to prevent exactly this scenario — teams selecting technologies based on short-term convenience rather than enterprise-level considerations. The missing enterprise SLAs, data residency gaps, and vendor lock-in are significant risks that the Innovation team is underweighting. The 4-month deadline pressure should not drive technology standardization decisions. Recommend that the Innovation team use the approved platforms and accept the 6-month delay, or find ways to accelerate delivery within the approved technology stack. The cloud-native platform can be evaluated for inclusion in the next annual standards review.

b) Grant a conditional dispensation with specific guardrails and an explicit time limit. Conditions: the platform may be used only for the specific product launch, not for other projects. The Innovation team must design the application to be portable (using containerization and abstraction layers that reduce lock-in). The product must not process data from the 3 countries without data residency coverage. The dispensation expires in 18 months, at which point the platform must either be added to the approved standards or the application must be migrated. Simultaneously, initiate an expedited evaluation to determine whether the platform should be added to the standards list, considering the industry trajectory.

c) Grant the dispensation unconditionally. The Technology Standards catalog should enable innovation, not block it. The 3x development velocity is a significant competitive advantage. Industry analysts predict the platform will become the standard within 3 years, so adding it to the approved list is likely inevitable. The enterprise SLAs and data residency gaps are the vendor's responsibility and will likely be resolved as the platform matures. Early adoption positions NovaTech to build expertise ahead of competitors. Document the dispensation decision with a note that the annual standards review should prioritize this platform for formal inclusion.

d) Defer the dispensation decision and propose an alternative: allow the Innovation team to use the cloud-native platform for a limited proof-of-concept (not full product launch) over 2 months. During this period, evaluate the platform's enterprise readiness, validate the 3x velocity claim with measured data, assess the actual vendor lock-in risk, and confirm data residency limitations. Based on the proof-of-concept results, either fast-track the platform into the approved standards or identify modifications needed. Negotiate with the Innovation team to adjust the product launch timeline by 2 months to accommodate this evaluation.

## Scenario 8: Designing an EA Measurement Framework from Scratch

Horizon Healthcare Network is a $12 billion healthcare system operating 20 hospitals, 200 clinics, and a health insurance plan serving 3 million members. The enterprise architecture program was established 4 years ago and has delivered several successful ADM cycles, guiding $600 million in technology investments. Despite these successes, the new CFO has challenged the Chief Architect: "You tell me architecture is valuable, but you can't show me a single metric that proves it. I want measurable evidence that the $8 million I spend on your team annually is a good investment."

The Chief Architect realizes that the EA program has never established formal measurement. Value claims have been anecdotal — "we prevented project X from failing," "we reduced technology complexity." No baseline metrics were established when the EA program began, making before/after comparisons difficult. The architecture team has been focused on delivery (ADM cycles, governance, standards) and has not invested in measurement.

The CFO has given the Chief Architect 90 days to propose an EA measurement framework and 6 months to begin reporting metrics. The Chief Architect must design a measurement approach that is credible to a finance executive, practical to implement, and genuinely reflects EA value. Complicating factors include: the EA team has no data analytics expertise, the company's project management tool doesn't track architecture-related metrics, and business stakeholders have mixed perceptions of EA value — some see the team as "essential governance" while others view them as "bureaucratic overhead."

### Question 8
Based on the scenario above, how should the Chief Architect design an EA measurement approach?

a) Design a balanced measurement framework with four categories aligned to TOGAF's EA value proposition: (1) Strategic Alignment — % of technology investments aligned with business strategy (measured through Architecture Board approval records); (2) Technology Efficiency — reduction in technology portfolio complexity (number of technology products, redundant applications, integration points); (3) Risk Management — number and value of architecture compliance issues identified and resolved before implementation; (4) Delivery Improvement — impact on project delivery (success rates, time-to-market, architecture reuse rates for projects that follow architecture versus those that don't). Establish baselines for each metric within 90 days and begin quarterly reporting. Present to the CFO as a dashboard with both leading and lagging indicators.

b) Focus exclusively on financial metrics that the CFO understands: (1) Cost avoidance — quantify technology investments that were redirected based on architecture recommendations (e.g., projects not started because architecture review identified redundancy); (2) Cost reduction — quantify savings from technology rationalization guided by architecture standards; (3) Risk reduction — quantify potential losses prevented by architecture governance (failed projects caught early, security vulnerabilities identified); (4) Architecture tax — calculate the cost of architecture governance (review time, compliance overhead) as a percentage of project costs to show it's proportionate. Engage finance team members to validate the methodology and ensure the numbers are credible to the CFO.

c) Commission a benchmarking study against comparable healthcare organizations. Identify 5-8 healthcare systems of similar size and complexity and compare: EA team size and budget as a percentage of IT spending, technology portfolio diversity, project success rates, technology spending per capita, and time-to-deploy new capabilities. This external benchmarking provides the CFO with context: "We spend $8 million, the industry average for our size is $10 million, and our technology efficiency metrics are in the top quartile." This approach is credible because it uses external reference points rather than self-reported value claims.

d) Propose a 6-month "EA Value Demonstration" pilot instead of a measurement framework. Select 3 upcoming technology initiatives and run them with two scenarios: one following the full architecture governance process and one following a lighter process. Compare outcomes across both approaches for metrics like cost, timeline, and quality. This A/B testing approach provides the most rigorous evidence of EA value because it controls for variables and demonstrates causation, not just correlation. Present the experimental design to the CFO within 90 days and begin the pilot immediately.

Protecting the $48 million already invested avoids sunk cost fallacy accusations while delivering promised capability (predictive maintenance). The parallel ADM cycle from Phase A for AI capabilities uses the TOGAF ADM's explicit support for parallel architecture cycles at different scopes. Deferring TA4 and TA5 decisions until the AI-focused Architecture Vision is complete avoids premature commitment to approaches that may be obsolete. This is the most nuanced response, using multiple TOGAF mechanisms (parallel ADM cycles, Transition Architecture preservation, strategic deferral) in concert.
- **Answer a) = 3 points (Second):** Triggering Phase H is procedurally correct — the AI disruption is exactly the type of technology change driver that Phase H monitors for. The impact analysis and option presentation are proper governance activities. However, the Phase H process may be too incremental for the scale of disruption described. Phase H is designed to assess whether the existing architecture needs modification — the AI disruption may require a fundamentally new Architecture Vision, not just roadmap modifications. The approach might produce a "patched" roadmap that tries to retrofit AI into a pre-AI architecture, rather than reconceiving the architecture for an AI-first world.
- **Answer b) = 1 point (Third):** Restarting the ADM from Phase A is conceptually sound but practically risky. Pausing TA3 at 60% completion wastes $48 million in investment and leaves factories without the promised predictive maintenance capability. - **Answer d) = 0 points (Distractor):** Escalating to the board for a fundamental program restructure bypasses the Architecture Board's governance authority and the ADM's change management processes.
