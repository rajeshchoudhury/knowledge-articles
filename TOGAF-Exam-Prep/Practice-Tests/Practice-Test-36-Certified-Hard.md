## Scenario 1: Mid-Flight Regulatory Disruption at a Global Bank

GlobalTrust Bank is a multinational financial institution operating in 35 countries with $800 billion in assets under management. Eighteen months ago, the bank launched "Project Horizon," a massive digital transformation initiative spanning retail banking, wealth management, and commercial lending. The Architecture Board approved an Architecture Vision in Phase A that focused on creating a unified digital platform to replace 14 legacy core banking systems. The program employed the TOGAF ADM, and the architecture team completed Phase B (Business Architecture) on schedule, producing a comprehensive Business Architecture that mapped 340 business processes across six business units.

The team is now deep into Phase C (Information Systems Architecture), having completed the Data Architecture and progressed 60% through the Application Architecture. At this critical juncture, the European Union has enacted the "Digital Financial Services Regulation" (DFSR), a sweeping new regulatory framework that fundamentally alters how banks must handle data residency, algorithmic transparency, and customer consent management. Simultaneously, three Asian markets where GlobalTrust operates have introduced conflicting data sovereignty laws.

The impact is severe: approximately 40% of the business requirements captured in Phase B are now either invalid or require significant modification. The approved Business Architecture's process models for customer onboarding, credit decisioning, and cross-border payments are no longer compliant. The data architecture completed in Phase C assumes cross-border data flows that are now prohibited under the new regulations. The CIO has stated publicly that "Project Horizon's timeline will not slip," and the program has already consumed $120 million of its $350 million budget. The Architecture Board meets in two weeks, and the Chief Architect must present a path forward. The compliance team has flagged 47 specific regulatory gaps, and the legal department warns that non-compliance penalties could reach $2.8 billion annually.

### Question 1
Based on the scenario above, what should the Chief Architect recommend to the Architecture Board?

a) Invoke the Architecture Change Management process in Phase H, formally document the regulatory changes as a major change request, reassess the Architecture Vision, and iterate back to Phase B to update the Business Architecture before proceeding. This ensures the architecture remains compliant and the ADM's iterative nature is properly leveraged, even though it will require a timeline adjustment.

b) Continue the current Phase C work but create a parallel workstream to develop a "Regulatory Overlay Architecture" that addresses the 47 compliance gaps as a separate Segment Architecture. Merge the two architectures during Phase E when creating the Implementation and Migration Plan. This approach preserves the current timeline while addressing compliance needs.

c) Immediately halt all Phase C work, escalate to the Architecture Board that the Architecture Vision from Phase A is no longer valid, and recommend restarting the ADM from Preliminary Phase to redefine the architecture principles and governance framework in light of the new regulatory landscape. Request a complete program re-baselining including budget and timeline.

d) Proceed with the current Phase C work as planned, but add regulatory compliance requirements as constraints to be addressed during Phase G (Implementation Governance) through architecture compliance reviews. Document the regulatory gaps as risks in the Architecture Risk Assessment and assign the compliance team to develop mitigation plans that can be implemented during solution delivery.

## Scenario 2: The CTO Who Wants to Skip Business Architecture

Nextera Technologies is a mid-sized technology company ($2 billion revenue) that manufactures IoT sensors for industrial applications. The company recently hired a new CTO, Marcus Chen, who was previously the VP of Engineering at a major cloud provider. Marcus has a strong technology background but limited experience with enterprise architecture frameworks. He is eager to modernize Nextera's technology stack, which he correctly identifies as outdated — the company still runs critical manufacturing systems on on-premises servers from 2012.

The enterprise architecture team, led by Chief Architect Sarah Okonkwo, has just completed the Preliminary Phase, establishing architecture principles and a governance framework. They are about to begin Phase A (Architecture Vision) for a major modernization initiative. During the Architecture Vision kickoff meeting, Marcus announces that he has already selected the target technology platform — a specific cloud provider's IoT-native infrastructure — and insists that the team skip Phase B (Business Architecture) entirely and move directly to Phase D (Technology Architecture). He argues that "we already know what the business does; what we don't know is how to fix the technology."

Marcus is politically powerful: he reports directly to the CEO, controls the $45 million technology modernization budget, and has the board of directors' confidence. Three of the seven Architecture Board members are his direct reports. Sarah's team of five architects is concerned because the business side has been demanding changes to manufacturing processes to support a new product line, and these requirements are undocumented. Furthermore, the company's acquisition of a German sensor manufacturer last year has introduced business processes that have never been formally analyzed. The CEO has privately told Sarah that he trusts Marcus's technical judgment but expects "proper governance."

### Question 2
Based on the scenario above, how should Chief Architect Sarah Okonkwo handle this situation?

a) Acknowledge Marcus's technology expertise and propose a compromise: conduct an accelerated Phase B that focuses specifically on the business capabilities and processes that directly impact the technology modernization. Use business capability modeling to quickly identify dependencies between business requirements and technology decisions, demonstrating the value of Phase B without delaying the program significantly. Engage Marcus as a key stakeholder in the Phase B outputs.

b) Formally present to the Architecture Board that skipping Phase B violates TOGAF's ADM methodology and the approved governance framework established in the Preliminary Phase. Cite the architecture principles that require business-driven technology decisions. Request the Architecture Board to mandate Phase B completion before any Phase D work begins. Document this as a governance compliance issue.

c) Agree to skip the full Phase B but insist on conducting a comprehensive stakeholder analysis and creating a Request for Architecture Work that documents all known business requirements, including those from the German acquisition. Use these as inputs directly to Phase D, with a commitment to validate business alignment during Phase E when Opportunities and Solutions are being evaluated.

d) Escalate the situation to the CEO, explaining that the CTO's approach creates significant risk. Recommend that the CEO direct Marcus to support the full ADM cycle. Present a risk assessment showing that skipping Phase B could result in technology investments that don't align with business needs, particularly given the undocumented German acquisition processes and the new product line requirements.

## Scenario 3: Non-Conformant Projects Under Immovable Deadlines

RegionalHealth is a large healthcare network operating 28 hospitals and 150 clinics across the southeastern United States. Two years ago, the organization embarked on a comprehensive healthcare IT transformation program called "CareBridge," designed to unify patient records, clinical workflows, and billing systems. The Architecture Board approved the target architecture, and Phase G (Implementation Governance) is now overseeing five critical implementation projects.

A scheduled compliance review has just been completed, revealing alarming results. Project Alpha (Electronic Health Records migration) is rated "Non-Conformant" because it has deviated from the approved data architecture by creating a separate patient identity database instead of using the enterprise master patient index. Project Gamma (Clinical Decision Support) is "Non-Conformant" because the development team adopted a different integration pattern than specified in the target application architecture, using point-to-point integrations instead of the mandated event-driven architecture. Project Delta (Revenue Cycle Management) is "Non-Conformant" because the vendor's SaaS solution cannot support the approved technology standards and the team has introduced unapproved middleware.

Projects Beta (Telehealth Platform) and Epsilon (Analytics & Reporting) are both rated "Conformant." The critical constraint is that RegionalHealth faces a CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) mandate requiring all five projects to be operational by March 31 of next year — just 7 months away. Missing this deadline means losing $180 million in annual Medicare reimbursements. Each non-conformant project is between 50-70% complete. The Architecture Board has emergency authority but has never dealt with this level of non-conformance simultaneously. The CFO is demanding that "nothing stops the deadline," while the Chief Architect insists that "architectural debt will cost more than the deadline."

### Question 3
Based on the scenario above, what governance action should the Architecture Board take?

a) Grant formal architecture dispensations for all three non-conformant projects with strict conditions: each project must document the deviations in an Architecture Compliance report, commit to a remediation plan with specific milestones for achieving conformance after the CMS deadline, and establish monitoring checkpoints. Create a Transition Architecture that incorporates the current non-conformant states as interim steps, with the next ADM iteration explicitly planning the convergence to target architecture.

b) Require all three non-conformant projects to halt development and immediately re-architect to conform to the target architecture. The Architecture Board should communicate to the CFO that architectural non-conformance creates patient safety risks and long-term costs that exceed the $180 million at stake. Commission an impact assessment showing the total cost of architectural debt versus the cost of the deadline miss.

c) Invoke the Architecture Change Management process to modify the target architecture to accommodate the deviations introduced by the three projects. If the projects have found workable solutions, the target architecture should be updated to reflect reality. This pragmatic approach avoids the fiction of "dispensations" and acknowledges that architecture must evolve based on implementation experience.

d) Separate the governance response by project risk level. Conduct an immediate technical debt analysis for each non-conformant project. For deviations that create interoperability or patient safety risks, require immediate remediation even if it impacts the timeline. For deviations that are purely architectural preferences with no functional impact, grant dispensations. Engage the CMS to negotiate a phased compliance approach.

## Scenario 4: Merging Two Enterprise Architectures Post-Acquisition

QuantumRetail, a major US-based e-commerce company ($15 billion revenue), has just acquired EuroShop, a European retail technology firm ($4 billion revenue), for $12 billion. The acquisition was driven by QuantumRetail's desire to enter the European market and leverage EuroShop's advanced AI-driven personalization technology. The deal closed 30 days ago, and the CEO has mandated "full operational integration within 18 months."

The challenge is that both companies have mature, well-established enterprise architectures that are fundamentally different. QuantumRetail's architecture is built on the TOGAF framework with a service-oriented architecture (SOA) pattern, using primarily AWS cloud services, Oracle databases, and Java-based microservices. Their Enterprise Continuum is well-populated with reference architectures and building blocks. EuroShop's architecture follows the Zachman Framework with a more monolithic application approach, running primarily on Microsoft Azure, SQL Server, and .NET technologies. Their documentation uses completely different taxonomies and notation.

Both companies have active Architecture Boards, established governance processes, and competing architecture principles. QuantumRetail's principles emphasize "cloud-native first" and "API-driven integration," while EuroShop's principles emphasize "data sovereignty" and "platform consolidation." The combined organization will operate under EU GDPR, US state privacy laws, and emerging regulations in both jurisdictions. There are 340 architects across both organizations (220 at QuantumRetail, 120 at EuroShop), and tensions are already emerging about whose architecture "wins." The CEO has appointed QuantumRetail's Chief Architect to lead the integration but has also stated that "we bought EuroShop for their technology, so don't throw it away." The Architecture Board must decide on an integration approach within 60 days.

### Question 4
Based on the scenario above, which ADM approach should the Chief Architect recommend for the enterprise architecture integration?

a) Start a fresh ADM cycle from the Preliminary Phase for the combined entity. Establish new architecture principles that reconcile both organizations' values, create a unified governance framework with representation from both Architecture Boards, and populate the Enterprise Continuum with building blocks from both organizations classified under a common taxonomy. Conduct Phase A to create a new Architecture Vision for the integrated enterprise, treating both existing architectures as the baseline.

b) Use the ADM's Architecture Partitioning approach to create a federated architecture. Maintain both enterprise architectures as separate Segment Architectures under a new overarching Strategic Architecture that defines shared principles, integration patterns, and governance. Use the Enterprise Continuum to classify and cross-reference building blocks from both organizations. Focus the initial ADM cycle on defining the Strategic Architecture and integration architecture only.

c) Adopt QuantumRetail's existing TOGAF-based architecture as the target architecture for the combined entity, since it is already TOGAF-aligned and represents the larger organization. Launch an ADM cycle starting at Phase B to extend the existing Business Architecture to incorporate EuroShop's business capabilities. Map EuroShop's Zachman artifacts to TOGAF equivalents and migrate their Architecture Repository content. Plan a phased technology migration from Azure to AWS.

d) Engage an external consulting firm to perform an independent architecture assessment of both organizations. Commission them to develop a "greenfield" target architecture that takes the best elements from both and isn't biased toward either company's existing approach. Use the consulting firm's output as the Architecture Vision for a new ADM cycle, ensuring objectivity in the integration approach.

## Scenario 5: Architecture Board vs. Chief Architect on Segment Architecture Scope

MetroPower is a large utility company serving 8 million customers across four states. The company is undergoing significant transformation driven by the transition to renewable energy, smart grid deployment, and the electrification of transportation. The Chief Architect, Dr. Elena Vasquez, has proposed a Segment Architecture for the "Customer Experience Transformation" initiative, which she has scoped to include customer engagement channels, billing systems, energy usage analytics, demand response programs, electric vehicle charging network management, and distributed energy resource (DER) integration.

The Architecture Board, chaired by the VP of Operations, disagrees with the scope. The board argues that the Segment Architecture is too broad and should be limited to customer-facing systems only (engagement channels and billing). They contend that DER integration and EV charging are "operational technology" concerns that belong in a separate Segment Architecture managed by the Grid Operations team. The VP of Operations has significant influence because utility operations are the company's core business and his division represents 70% of the workforce.

Dr. Vasquez argues that modern utility customers increasingly interact with DERs (solar panels, battery storage) and EV charging through the same digital platforms they use for billing and outage reporting. Separating these domains would create artificial boundaries that result in fragmented customer experiences. She has data showing that 35% of customer service calls are already related to DER and EV integration issues, and the company's Net Promoter Score has dropped 18 points in the last year due to disjointed digital experiences.

The Strategic Architecture, approved six months ago, is ambiguous on this point — it lists "Customer Transformation" and "Grid Modernization" as separate architecture segments but doesn't define their boundaries clearly. Three upcoming projects worth $85 million are waiting for this scope decision before proceeding.

### Question 5
Based on the scenario above, how should this scope disagreement be resolved?

a) Defer to the Architecture Board's decision since they are the governing body responsible for architecture scope decisions per TOGAF governance framework. The Chief Architect should accept the narrower scope, develop the Customer Experience Segment Architecture for customer-facing systems only, and recommend that the Grid Operations team develop a parallel Segment Architecture for DER and EV charging. Establish integration requirements between the two segments.

b) Propose that the disagreement be resolved by revisiting the Strategic Architecture to explicitly define Segment Architecture boundaries. Commission a stakeholder analysis and business capability mapping exercise that objectively determines which capabilities are customer-domain versus operational-domain. Use the capability map to drive the scope decision based on business architecture evidence rather than organizational politics. Present this to the Architecture Board as a data-driven recommendation.

c) Escalate the disagreement to the executive sponsor (CEO or equivalent) since the Architecture Board and Chief Architect have reached an impasse. Per TOGAF governance principles, the Chief Architect has a responsibility to advocate for architectural integrity, and if the governing body makes a decision that compromises the architecture, the architect must escalate. The executive sponsor should make the final scope determination.

d) Accept the Architecture Board's narrower scope for the initial Segment Architecture but include DER and EV charging integration points as explicit architecture requirements. Define the interfaces and data exchanges needed between the Customer Experience and Grid Operations segments in the Architecture Definition Document. This creates a practical path forward while ensuring the customer experience vision isn't compromised by the separation.

## Scenario 6: Establishing Federated Governance Across 15 Government Departments

The Federal Ministry of Digital Transformation has been mandated by the Prime Minister to establish enterprise architecture governance across 15 government departments, ranging from Defense (120,000 employees, $80 billion budget) to the Department of Cultural Affairs (2,000 employees, $500 million budget). Currently, each department operates independently: four departments have mature EA practices using TOGAF, three use the Federal Enterprise Architecture Framework (FEAF), two use DoDAF (Defense-specific), and six have no formal architecture practice at all.

The Ministry has appointed a Chief Government Architect (CGA) with a team of 12 architects and a mandate to "establish coherent enterprise architecture governance within 24 months." The CGA has no direct authority over department CIOs — she can only recommend, not mandate. The political environment is complex: a national election is expected in 18 months, department heads are political appointees who resist perceived centralization, and a recent failed IT mega-project ($3 billion wasted) has made politicians wary of centralized technology programs.

Key challenges include: data sharing between departments is critically needed for citizen services (e.g., a citizen applying for benefits shouldn't need to provide the same information to five departments), but departments cite security and jurisdictional concerns. Technology procurement is fragmented — 15 departments have 15 different procurement processes, resulting in $200 million annually in redundant IT spending. There is no shared Architecture Repository, no common principles, and no enterprise-level governance body. The Prime Minister's office wants visible results within 6 months to demonstrate progress before election season begins.

### Question 6
Based on the scenario above, how should the Chief Government Architect establish a federated governance model?

a) Begin with the Preliminary Phase tailored for the government context: establish an Architecture Board with CIO representation from all 15 departments, define common architecture principles, and create a shared Architecture Repository. Use the TOGAF Architecture Governance framework to define clear roles, responsibilities, and decision rights across federal and departmental levels. Start a Capability-Based Planning exercise to identify shared capabilities across departments. Pursue all 15 departments simultaneously to ensure equity.

b) Adopt a phased federation approach: first, form a voluntary "Coalition of the Willing" with the four TOGAF-mature departments. Establish a Federal Architecture Board with these departments, define shared principles and standards that these departments agree to, and deliver quick wins (shared reference architectures, common building blocks) within 6 months. Use the Enterprise Continuum to create a Federal Foundation Architecture that departments can adopt incrementally. Expand governance to other departments based on demonstrated value, adapting onboarding for departments using FEAF and DoDAF.

c) Focus the first 6 months entirely on creating a comprehensive Federal Enterprise Architecture framework document that synthesizes TOGAF, FEAF, and DoDAF into a unified government architecture methodology. Present this to the Prime Minister as tangible progress. Then mandate adoption through a cabinet directive requiring all departments to comply within 18 months. Use compliance audits to enforce adoption.

d) Commission an Architecture Maturity Assessment of all 15 departments using the TOGAF Architecture Maturity Model. Based on the results, create department-specific roadmaps for architecture capability development. Establish the Federal Architecture Board immediately but give it advisory-only authority initially. Focus on creating shared infrastructure services (e.g., identity management, data exchange platform) that provide immediate value without requiring departments to change their architecture practices.

## Scenario 7: Five Transition Architectures, Budget for Only Three

Oceanic Airlines is a major carrier operating 350 aircraft, serving 120 destinations, and employing 45,000 people. Phase E (Opportunities and Solutions) of their digital transformation ADM cycle has produced a comprehensive target architecture that requires the airline to move from its current state to a fully digital, customer-centric operating model. The architecture team has determined that five Transition Architectures are needed to manage the complexity and risk of this transformation.

Transition Architecture 1 (TA1): Unified Data Platform — consolidate 23 operational databases into a single data lake/warehouse, enabling real-time analytics. Cost: $28 million. Enables TA2, TA3, and TA5. Transition Architecture 2 (TA2): Digital Customer Experience — replace the 15-year-old reservation and loyalty systems with modern cloud-native platforms. Cost: $45 million. Depends on TA1. Revenue impact: estimated $120 million annual revenue increase. Transition Architecture 3 (TA3): Predictive Operations — implement AI-driven flight operations, crew scheduling, and maintenance prediction. Cost: $35 million. Depends on TA1. Operational savings: estimated $90 million annually. Transition Architecture 4 (TA4): Modern Workforce Platform — replace HR, training, and crew management systems. Cost: $22 million. Independent of other TAs. Employee satisfaction impact: critical for retaining pilots amid industry shortage. Transition Architecture 5 (TA5): Regulatory Compliance Automation — automate compliance reporting for 40 aviation authorities worldwide. Cost: $18 million. Depends on TA1. Regulatory risk: current manual processes have resulted in $12 million in fines over 3 years.

Total cost for all five: $148 million. Available budget: $85 million over 3 years. The CFO has stated the budget is firm. The Architecture Board must decide which Transition Architectures to implement and in what order, knowing that the full target architecture cannot be achieved within the current funding cycle.

### Question 7
Based on the scenario above, how should the Architecture Board prioritize the Transition Architectures?

a) Select TA1, TA2, and TA3 ($108 million) and request the additional $23 million from the CFO by presenting the combined ROI case: $210 million in annual revenue increase and operational savings against $108 million in investment. Defer TA4 and TA5 to a future funding cycle. This maximizes financial return and ensures the foundational data platform enables the highest-value capabilities.

b) Select TA1, TA4, and TA5 ($68 million) within the budget, leaving $17 million as contingency. TA1 is the foundation that enables future phases. TA5 addresses regulatory risk that could result in operational shutdowns, not just fines. TA4 addresses the critical pilot retention crisis. Defer TA2 and TA3 to the next funding cycle when TA1 will be complete, making them easier and cheaper to implement.

c) Select TA1, TA2, and TA5 ($91 million) and negotiate with the CFO for the additional $6 million by presenting the regulatory risk mitigation of TA5 and the revenue generation of TA2. TA1 provides the foundation. TA2 delivers the highest single revenue impact. TA5 mitigates regulatory risk and potential operational disruption. Defer TA3 and TA4. Create architecture roadmap showing how deferred TAs will be addressed in the next ADM cycle.

d) Propose restructuring the Transition Architectures to fit the budget. Combine elements of TA1 and TA5 into a single "Data and Compliance Foundation" transition ($38 million). Descope TA2 to focus only on the reservation system replacement ($25 million), deferring loyalty system modernization. Implement TA4 as planned ($22 million). Total: $85 million within budget. This approach addresses the broadest set of stakeholder concerns while staying within fiscal constraints, acknowledging that the original target architecture scope must be adjusted.

## Scenario 8: Enterprise Continuum Misuse as a Repository

TechForward Solutions is a rapidly growing technology consulting firm ($3 billion revenue) with 8,000 consultants globally. Three years ago, the company established an enterprise architecture practice and adopted TOGAF. The architecture team enthusiastically embraced the Enterprise Continuum concept but has fundamentally misunderstood its purpose. Instead of using it as a classification and navigation framework for architectural assets, they have built an elaborate custom software platform called "Continuum Hub" that they use as the organization's primary Architecture Repository.

The "Continuum Hub" stores everything — architecture artifacts, project documents, governance records, compliance reports, vendor assessments, meeting minutes, and even individual architects' working notes — organized loosely into categories labeled "Foundation," "Common Systems," "Industry," and "Organization-Specific." There are over 45,000 items in the system with no consistent metadata, no version control, no governance around what gets published, and no clear distinction between reference architectures, building blocks, and implementation artifacts.

The consequences are severe: architects spend an average of 3 hours per week searching for relevant reference materials and rarely find them. New architects receive no guidance on how to navigate the system. The same building block has been independently recreated 14 times because architects couldn't find existing versions. The architecture practice's credibility is suffering because stakeholders perceive the team as "disorganized and unable to manage their own tools." The CIO has threatened to cancel funding for the architecture practice unless the situation is resolved within 6 months. The platform itself cost $2 million to build and the team that created it is resistant to change, viewing any restructuring as criticism of their work.

### Question 8
Based on the scenario above, how should the enterprise architecture team correct the Enterprise Continuum misuse?

a) Redesign the architecture practice's information management by properly separating the Enterprise Continuum from the Architecture Repository. Re-establish the Enterprise Continuum as a classification scheme with clear taxonomy levels (Foundation, Common Systems, Industry, Organization-Specific) that classifies and provides navigational context for architectural assets. Separately structure the Architecture Repository with proper components: Architecture Metamodel, Architecture Landscape, Standards Information Base, Reference Library, and Governance Log. Migrate and curate the 45,000 items, discarding non-architectural content.

b) Hire an external consultancy to audit the "Continuum Hub" and recommend a commercial off-the-shelf Architecture Repository tool that properly implements TOGAF's repository concepts. Migrate all content to the new tool, using the migration as an opportunity to classify and curate content. Retire the custom platform. This provides an objective external perspective and avoids internal political conflicts about who built the current system.

c) Keep the "Continuum Hub" platform but restructure its content organization. Create a proper governance process for content publication, implement version control, establish metadata standards, and assign content stewards for each classification category. Add a search capability that uses the Enterprise Continuum classification as a navigation taxonomy. This approach preserves the $2 million investment and avoids the disruption of migrating to a new system.

d) Conduct a thorough education program first: train all architects on the proper TOGAF concepts of Enterprise Continuum versus Architecture Repository. Then form a working group with representatives from the team that built "Continuum Hub" to redesign the information architecture. Implement the Architecture Repository structure defined by TOGAF with proper separation of concerns. Curate and migrate content in phases, starting with the most-used building blocks and reference architectures.
