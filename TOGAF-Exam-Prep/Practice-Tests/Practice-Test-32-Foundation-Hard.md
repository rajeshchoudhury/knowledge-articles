## Answer Key

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** b) Elements that exist in both the Baseline and Target but require modification  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Gap Analysis Technique  
**Explanation:** While new elements (gaps) and eliminated elements are important to identify, elements that exist in both architectures but require modification demand the most careful attention. These are the most easily overlooked because they appear to be "covered" in both states but actually need transformation work. Simple additions or deletions are relatively straightforward to identify, but understanding what changes are needed to existing components—and the implications of those changes—requires deep analysis of both the current and target states.

---

### Question 2
**Correct Answer:** a) Keep Satisfied — ensure their needs are met without overwhelming them with detail  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Stakeholder Power/Interest Grid  
**Explanation:** In the Power/Interest Grid, stakeholders with HIGH power and LOW interest should be managed with a "Keep Satisfied" strategy. Because they have significant influence over the project but limited day-to-day interest, the architect should ensure their needs are met and avoid causing dissatisfaction, but should not overwhelm them with excessive detail that might trigger unnecessary interference. "Manage Closely" (option b) is for HIGH power/HIGH interest. "Monitor" (option c) is for LOW power/LOW interest. "Keep Informed" (option d) is for LOW power/HIGH interest.

---

### Question 3
**Correct Answer:** b) IT infrastructure current market value and depreciation schedule  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Business Transformation Readiness Assessment  
**Explanation:** The Business Transformation Readiness Assessment evaluates organizational readiness across multiple factors including vision clarity, executive sponsorship, organizational capacity to absorb change, business case viability, and resource availability. IT infrastructure market value and depreciation schedules are financial accounting concerns, not readiness factors for business transformation. While IT capacity and readiness are assessed, this is about capability to support change—not about asset valuation or depreciation, which belong in financial management rather than transformation readiness.

---

### Question 4
**Correct Answer:** a) Phase E — to evaluate factors that impact the architecture implementation and determine deductions from the ideal migration approach  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Implementation Factor Assessment and Deduction Matrix  
**Explanation:** The Implementation Factor Assessment and Deduction Matrix is used in Phase E (Opportunities and Migration Planning) to evaluate various factors (costs, risks, benefits, dependencies) that impact the implementation approach and make deductions from the theoretical ideal migration plan. These deductions reflect real-world constraints that may require adjustments to sequencing or approach. It is not a Phase F detailed planning tool (option b), not a Phase G compliance tool (option c), and not a Phase H post-implementation assessment (option d).

It is not about product selection (option a), accountability assignment (option c), or cost calculation (option d).

**Explanation:** The key distinction is temporal and relates to mitigation: initial risk assessment identifies and classifies risks before any mitigation actions are applied, while residual risk assessment evaluates what risks remain after mitigation strategies have been planned or implemented. Both address all types of risk, not just business or technical (option d is wrong). This distinction helps architects understand whether their mitigation strategies are effective.

**Explanation:** In the TOGAF Gap Analysis matrix, rows typically represent Baseline Architecture elements (what exists today) and columns represent Target Architecture elements (what is needed in the future). It is not a capability-technology mapping (option a), phase-deliverable mapping (option c), or stakeholder-concern mapping (option d).

### Question 10
**Correct Answer:** a) Operational interoperability — shared business processes and shared management of business functions  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Interoperability Requirements  
**Explanation:** TOGAF defines interoperability levels from lowest to highest sophistication as: Technical (basic connectivity) → Syntactic (agreed data format) → Semantic (agreed data meaning) → Operational (shared business processes). Operational interoperability is the highest level because it goes beyond data exchange to include shared business processes, procedures, and management of business functions across organizational boundaries. This represents the most sophisticated form of interoperability, as it requires alignment not just in technology and data but in business operations.

### Question 11
**Correct Answer:** d) Cost-Prohibitive — the implementation cannot conform due to budget constraints  
**Difficulty:** Expert  
**Topic:** Architecture Compliance Review Categories  
**Explanation:** "Cost-Prohibitive" is NOT a standard compliance finding category in TOGAF's Architecture Compliance review process. The standard categories include: Irrelevant (standard doesn't apply), Consistent (implementation conforms), Conformant (implementation conforms with an approved interpretation), Non-Conformant (implementation deviates without approval), and others like "Not Applicable." Cost issues would be addressed through the dispensation process if the architecture cannot be fully implemented.

### Question 15

**Explanation:** These are complementary parts of the same technique used in Phase E. The Implementation Factor Assessment identifies and evaluates factors such as costs, benefits, risks, and business value that affect implementation projects. The Deduction Matrix then uses these factors to make deductions (adjustments) to the relative priority and sequencing of projects, helping determine the optimal migration approach. They are parts of the same technique, not separate techniques at different phases (option b). Both can use qualitative and quantitative methods (option c is wrong). "Deduction" refers to logical reasoning about priorities, not budget subtraction (option d is wrong).

While these concepts are similar to the generic "Probability and Impact" (option a), TOGAF uses the specific terminology of "Effect" and "Frequency" classifications. Options b and d describe dimensions not used in TOGAF's risk classification framework.

**Explanation:** In TOGAF's Architecture Compliance context, a dispensation is a formally approved deviation from the architecture standard that is granted before or during implementation. It acknowledges that a specific project has legitimate reasons to deviate and has received governance approval to do so. An exception is a deviation discovered during a compliance review that was not previously approved. This distinction is important for governance—dispensations represent controlled flexibility while exceptions represent potential compliance failures that need resolution.
