# TOGAF 10 ADM Phases Flashcards

> 120+ exam-focused flashcards covering every ADM phase in detail.
> Format: **Q** = Question, **A** = Answer

---

## 1. ADM Overview

**Q:** What does ADM stand for?
**A:** Architecture Development Method.

---

**Q:** How many phases does the ADM have?
**A:** 10 phases: Preliminary Phase, Phases A through H, and Requirements Management (at the center).

---

**Q:** List all ADM phases in order.
**A:** Preliminary → A (Architecture Vision) → B (Business Architecture) → C (Information Systems Architectures) → D (Technology Architecture) → E (Opportunities & Solutions) → F (Migration Planning) → G (Implementation Governance) → H (Architecture Change Management) → Requirements Management (continuous, at center).

---

**Q:** Is the ADM a linear process?
**A:** No. The ADM is iterative and cyclic. Each phase can revisit previous phases, and the entire cycle can be repeated.

---

**Q:** What sits at the center of the ADM cycle?
**A:** Requirements Management — it interacts with and supports all other phases continuously throughout the cycle.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between an ADM iteration and an ADM cycle?
**A:** A cycle is a complete pass through all ADM phases. An iteration is a partial pass, potentially going through only some phases or revisiting specific phases as needed.

---

**Q:** What are the four iteration approaches for the ADM?
**A:** (1) Architecture Context iteration — across the entire ADM, (2) Architecture Definition iteration — Phases B, C, D, (3) Transition Planning iteration — Phases E, F, (4) Architecture Governance iteration — Phases G, H.

---

**Q:** What triggers a new ADM cycle?
**A:** A significant change in business strategy, technology landscape, or regulatory environment that requires a new or updated enterprise architecture.

---

## 2. Preliminary Phase

**Q:** What is the objective of the Preliminary Phase?
**A:** To prepare the organization for successful architecture work. It defines the architecture framework, principles, tools, governance structure, and scope of the architecture capability.

---

**Q:** Is the Preliminary Phase done once or repeated?
**A:** Typically done once to establish the architecture capability, then revisited as needed to update governance, principles, or organizational structures.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to the Preliminary Phase?
**A:** TOGAF Library, other architecture frameworks, board strategies, business principles/goals/drivers, governance and legal frameworks, existing organizational model for EA, existing architecture framework (if any).

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of the Preliminary Phase?
**A:** Organizational Model for Enterprise Architecture, Tailored Architecture Framework, Architecture Principles, Architecture Repository (initial), Architecture Governance framework, Request for Architecture Work (for Phase A).

---

**Q:** What is "tailoring" in the Preliminary Phase?
**A:** Adapting the TOGAF framework and ADM to fit the specific needs, culture, and maturity of the organization. This includes selecting which deliverables, artifacts, and phases are relevant.

---

**Q:** What organizational structures are defined in the Preliminary Phase?
**A:** The architecture organization (architecture team), roles and responsibilities, Architecture Board structure, and governance processes.

---

**Q:** What is the Architecture Governance framework established in this phase?
**A:** The policies, procedures, organization structures, and support to ensure architecture work is properly managed, including compliance reviews and decision-making authority.

---

**Q:** What is the "Request for Architecture Work"?
**A:** A document from the sponsoring organization that triggers the start of an ADM cycle. It identifies the business reasons for the architecture work and the desired outcomes.

---

**Q:** What tool/technique is especially associated with the Preliminary Phase?
**A:** Architecture Principles definition. Principles are created and documented using the Name–Statement–Rationale–Implications template.

---

## 3. Phase A — Architecture Vision

**Q:** What is the objective of Phase A?
**A:** To develop a high-level aspirational vision of the capabilities and business value to be delivered, secure formal approval and commitment from stakeholders, and create the Statement of Architecture Work.

---

**Q:** What triggers Phase A?
**A:** A Request for Architecture Work issued in the Preliminary Phase or from a recognized sponsoring organization.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to Phase A?
**A:** Request for Architecture Work, Architecture Principles, Enterprise Continuum, Architecture Repository content, business strategy/goals/drivers, Architecture Governance framework.

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of Phase A?
**A:** Architecture Vision document, Statement of Architecture Work (approved), refined stakeholder map, Communication Plan, high-level baseline and target architectures (all four domains, at high level).

---

**Q:** What is the Statement of Architecture Work?
**A:** A formal agreement between the architecture team and the sponsor that defines the scope, approach, schedule, and resources for the architecture project. It is essentially the project charter for the architecture engagement.

---

**Q:** What is the Architecture Vision document?
**A:** A high-level summary of the changes to the enterprise that will be achieved. It articulates the problem statement, objective, business scenarios, high-level stakeholder requirements, and a solution overview.

---

**Q:** What technique is strongly associated with Phase A?
**A:** Business Scenarios — a technique for understanding and documenting business requirements by analyzing real business situations.

---

**Q:** What is a Business Scenario?
**A:** A complete description of a business problem or opportunity, including the business and technology environment, the business process, the actors, and the desired outcome. Used to derive architecture requirements and evaluate architecture solutions.

---

**Q:** When is stakeholder identification and analysis primarily done?
**A:** Phase A. A stakeholder map/matrix is created, identifying key stakeholders, their concerns, and their influence/power levels.

---

**Q:** What approval is obtained at the end of Phase A?
**A:** Approval of the Statement of Architecture Work by the sponsor and key stakeholders, authorizing the architecture development effort.

---

**Q:** Does Phase A produce detailed architectures?
**A:** No. Phase A produces only high-level, aspirational views of the architecture across all four domains. Detailed architecture work happens in Phases B, C, and D.

---

**Q:** What is the "value proposition" created in Phase A?
**A:** A clear articulation of the business value the architecture project will deliver, essential for securing stakeholder buy-in and sponsorship.

---

---

## 4. Phase B — Business Architecture

**Q:** What is the objective of Phase B?
**A:** To develop the Business Architecture that describes how the enterprise needs to operate to achieve the business goals, and to identify the business architecture components of the Architecture Roadmap.

---

**Q:** What does Phase B describe?
**A:** The baseline and target Business Architectures, including business strategy, governance, organization, and key business processes.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to Phase B?
**A:** Architecture Vision (from Phase A), Architecture Principles, Enterprise Continuum, Architecture Repository, Request for Architecture Work, business strategy/goals/drivers, gap analysis results (if iterating).

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of Phase B?
**A:** Refined Architecture Vision, Draft Business Architecture (baseline and target), Business Architecture components of the Architecture Roadmap, Gap Analysis results, updated Architecture Requirements Specification.

---

**Q:** What key artifacts (catalogs, matrices, diagrams) are created in Phase B?
**A:** Catalogs: Organization/Actor Catalog, Driver/Goal/Objective Catalog, Role Catalog, Business Service/Function Catalog, Location Catalog, Process/Event/Control/Product Catalog, Contract/Measure Catalog. Matrices: Business Interaction Matrix, Actor/Role Matrix. Diagrams: Business Footprint Diagram, Business Service/Information Diagram, Functional Decomposition Diagram, Product Lifecycle Diagram.

---

**Q:** What is Gap Analysis?
**A:** A technique used (first formally in Phase B, then in C and D) to compare the baseline architecture with the target architecture to identify gaps (missing capabilities), overlaps, and elements to be removed or carried forward.

---

**Q:** What modeling techniques can be used in Phase B?
**A:** Activity models, use-case models, process models (BPMN), class models, business scenarios. TOGAF does not mandate a specific modeling language.

---

**Q:** In Phase B, what is a Functional Decomposition Diagram?
**A:** A diagram that breaks down the enterprise's functions into sub-functions, providing a hierarchical view of business capabilities.

---

---

## 5. Phase C — Information Systems Architectures

**Q:** What is the objective of Phase C?
**A:** To develop the Data Architecture and Application Architecture that enable the Business Architecture and the Architecture Vision.

---

**Q:** What are the two sub-architectures developed in Phase C?
**A:** Data Architecture and Application Architecture.

---

**Q:** Can Data Architecture and Application Architecture in Phase C be developed in any order?
**A:** Yes. TOGAF allows them to be developed in either order, or concurrently. The choice depends on the organization's priorities and context.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to Phase C?
**A:** Architecture Vision, Business Architecture outputs (Phase B), Architecture Principles, Enterprise Continuum, Architecture Repository, gap analysis from Phase B.

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of Phase C?
**A:** Draft Data Architecture (baseline and target), Draft Application Architecture (baseline and target), Data and Application components of the Architecture Roadmap, Gap Analysis results for Data and Application, updated Architecture Requirements Specification.

---

### Phase C — Data Architecture

**Q:** What does the Data Architecture describe?
**A:** The structure of the organization's logical and physical data assets and data management resources — data entities, data relationships, data migration rules, data governance policies.

---

**Q:** What key artifacts are created for Data Architecture?
**A:** Catalogs: Data Entity/Data Component Catalog. Matrices: Data Entity/Business Function Matrix, Application/Data Matrix. Diagrams: Conceptual Data Diagram, Logical Data Diagram, Data Dissemination Diagram, Data Security Diagram, Data Migration Diagram, Data Lifecycle Diagram.

---

**Q:** What is a Data Entity/Business Function Matrix?
**A:** A matrix that maps data entities to the business functions that create, read, update, or delete (CRUD) them.

---

### Phase C — Application Architecture

**Q:** What does the Application Architecture describe?
**A:** A blueprint of individual application systems, their interactions, and their relationships to core business processes.

---

**Q:** What key artifacts are created for Application Architecture?
**A:** Catalogs: Application Portfolio Catalog, Interface Catalog. Matrices: Application/Organization Matrix, Role/Application Matrix, Application/Function Matrix, Application Interaction Matrix. Diagrams: Application Communication Diagram, Application and User Location Diagram, Application Use-Case Diagram, Enterprise Manageability Diagram, Process/Application Realization Diagram, Software Engineering Diagram, Application Migration Diagram, Software Distribution Diagram.

---

**Q:** What is an Application Portfolio Catalog?
**A:** A list of all applications in the enterprise, their key attributes (owner, technology, lifecycle status), used to understand the application landscape.

---

**Q:** What is an Application Interaction Matrix?
**A:** A matrix that shows the communication and data exchange relationships between applications.

---

---

## 6. Phase D — Technology Architecture

**Q:** What is the objective of Phase D?
**A:** To develop the Technology Architecture that maps application and data components to technology components, describing the IT infrastructure needed to support the deployment of the architectures developed in Phases B and C.

---

**Q:** What does the Technology Architecture describe?
**A:** The logical and physical technology infrastructure — hardware, software, networking, middleware, processing capabilities, and standards required to support the application and data architectures.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to Phase D?
**A:** Architecture Vision, Business Architecture (Phase B), Data and Application Architectures (Phase C), Architecture Principles, Enterprise Continuum, Architecture Repository, gap analysis results from prior phases.

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of Phase D?
**A:** Draft Technology Architecture (baseline and target), Technology Architecture components of the Architecture Roadmap, Gap Analysis results, updated Architecture Requirements Specification.

---

**Q:** What key artifacts are created in Phase D?
**A:** Catalogs: Technology Standards Catalog, Technology Portfolio Catalog. Matrices: Application/Technology Matrix. Diagrams: Environments and Locations Diagram, Platform Decomposition Diagram, Processing Diagram, Networked Computing/Hardware Diagram, Communications Engineering Diagram.

---

**Q:** What is a Platform Decomposition Diagram?
**A:** A diagram that shows the technology platform supporting the operations of the Information Systems Architecture — depicting the technology components and their relationships.

---

**Q:** What is the Technology Standards Catalog?
**A:** A catalog that documents the technology standards applicable to the architecture, including mandated products, versions, and lifecycle status.

---

**Q:** After Phase D, what major deliverable is substantially complete?
**A:** The Architecture Definition Document — containing the baseline and target architectures across all four domains (Business, Data, Application, Technology).

---

---

## 7. Phase E — Opportunities and Solutions

**Q:** What is the objective of Phase E?
**A:** To generate the initial, complete version of the Architecture Roadmap based on gap analysis performed in Phases B–D, identify major implementation projects, group them into Transition Architectures, and evaluate business value.

---

**Q:** What distinguishes Phase E from the earlier phases?
**A:** Phase E shifts from architecture definition to implementation planning. It identifies work packages, projects, and solution building blocks rather than defining architecture content.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to Phase E?
**A:** Architecture Definition Document (baseline and target for all domains), Gap Analysis results from B/C/D, Architecture Requirements Specification, Architecture Roadmap (draft components from B/C/D), candidate Architecture Roadmap components.

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of Phase E?
**A:** Architecture Roadmap (consolidated), Transition Architectures, Implementation and Migration Strategy, Implementation and Migration Plan (initial), work packages and projects identified, updated Architecture Requirements Specification.

---

**Q:** What is a Transition Architecture?
**A:** An intermediate architecture state between the baseline and target, representing a measurable, planned milestone in the transformation journey.

---

**Q:** What are the factors assessed in Phase E?
**A:** Implementation Factor Assessment and Deduction matrix — factors such as risks, costs, business value, dependencies, organizational readiness, and resource availability.

---

**Q:** What technique helps determine project groupings in Phase E?
**A:** Consolidated Gap Analysis across all architecture domains (B, C, D) combined with business value assessment, risk assessment, and dependency analysis.

---

**Q:** What is the difference between the Architecture Roadmap and the Implementation and Migration Plan?
**A:** The Architecture Roadmap is a list of individual work packages showing the evolution from baseline to target. The Implementation and Migration Plan is a detailed, time-sequenced schedule of projects that implements the roadmap.

---

**Q:** Where are Solution Building Blocks (SBBs) first identified?
**A:** Phase E — Opportunities and Solutions. SBBs represent the actual products, tools, and implementations that realize the Architecture Building Blocks.

---

---

## 8. Phase F — Migration Planning

**Q:** What is the objective of Phase F?
**A:** To finalize a detailed Implementation and Migration Plan that coordinates the migration from the baseline to the target architecture through the defined Transition Architectures.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to Phase F?
**A:** Architecture Roadmap (from Phase E), Transition Architectures, Implementation Factor Assessment, Implementation and Migration Strategy, Architecture Definition Document, Architecture Requirements Specification.

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of Phase F?
**A:** Finalized Implementation and Migration Plan, finalized Architecture Roadmap, finalized Transition Architectures, Architecture Definition Document (finalized), reusable Architecture Building Blocks, implementation project charters/requests.

---

**Q:** What is prioritization based on in Phase F?
**A:** Business value, risk, resource availability, project interdependencies, organizational readiness, and quick wins. A cost/benefit analysis is performed for each project.

---

**Q:** What technique is used in Phase F to assess business value?
**A:** Business Value Assessment — evaluating each project's contribution to business goals, strategic alignment, ROI, and stakeholder impact.

---

**Q:** What is the Implementation Factor Assessment and Deduction matrix used in Phase F?
**A:** A matrix that catalogs factors (risks, issues, assumptions, dependencies, business impacts) for each work package, enabling prioritization and sequencing of migration projects.

---

**Q:** What coordination activity is important in Phase F?
**A:** Coordinating the Architecture Roadmap with the organization's overall project portfolio management, ensuring alignment with existing programs and resource constraints.

---

**Q:** What approval is typically obtained at the end of Phase F?
**A:** Management approval of the Implementation and Migration Plan, authorizing the transition to implementation governance.

---

---

## 9. Phase G — Implementation Governance

**Q:** What is the objective of Phase G?
**A:** To provide architectural oversight of the implementation, ensure conformance of implementation projects with the target architecture, and govern the realization of the Architecture Roadmap.

---

**Q:** What is the primary role of the architecture team in Phase G?
**A:** Governance — ensuring that implementation projects deliver solutions that are consistent with and conform to the defined architecture.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to Phase G?
**A:** Implementation and Migration Plan, Architecture Definition Document, Architecture Requirements Specification, Architecture Contracts, Architecture Compliance review results, implementation project plans.

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of Phase G?
**A:** Architecture Contracts (signed), Compliance Assessments, change requests for architecture updates, recommendations for architecture/implementation changes, Architecture-compliant implemented solutions.

---

**Q:** What is an Architecture Contract?
**A:** A joint agreement between development partners and sponsors on the deliverables, quality, and fitness-for-purpose of an architecture. It governs the entire process of architecture development, implementation, and deployment.

---

**Q:** What are the two types of Architecture Contracts?
**A:** (1) Contracts between the architecture function and the business — governing architecture development. (2) Contracts between the architecture function and implementation projects — governing implementation conformance.

---

**Q:** What is an Architecture Compliance review?
**A:** A formal assessment of whether an implementation project or deployed solution conforms to the defined architecture. It identifies deviations and their severity.

---

**Q:** What are the possible outcomes of a Compliance Review?
**A:** Irrelevant, Consistent, Compliant, Conformant, Fully Conformant, Non-Conformant. (See Governance flashcards for details on each level.)

---

**Q:** Who typically performs Architecture Compliance reviews?
**A:** The Architecture Board or a designated architecture review team, with input from project teams and stakeholders.

---

**Q:** What happens if a project is found to be non-conformant?
**A:** A dispensation may be granted (if the deviation is justified), the project may be required to remediate, or a change request may be raised to update the architecture.

---

---

## 10. Phase H — Architecture Change Management

**Q:** What is the objective of Phase H?
**A:** To establish and support an architecture change management process that ensures the architecture remains current and responsive to changes in the business and technology environment.

---

**Q:** When does Phase H become active?
**A:** After the initial architecture has been deployed and is in operation. Phase H is an ongoing, continuous activity.

---

**Q:** What are the key inputs to Phase H?
**A:** Change requests (from Phase G, operations, business, technology changes), Architecture Compliance review results, Architecture Performance metrics, deployed architecture.

---

**Q:** What are the key outputs of Phase H?
**A:** Architecture updates, new Request for Architecture Work (if changes require a new ADM cycle), changes to the Architecture Framework and Principles (if needed), updated Architecture Repository.

---

**Q:** What are the three categories of architecture change?
**A:** (1) Simplification changes — can be handled within the current ADM cycle through streamlining. (2) Incremental changes — may require a partial ADM cycle. (3) Re-architecting changes — require a full new ADM cycle due to the scale of changes.

---

**Q:** What triggers a new ADM cycle from Phase H?
**A:** Re-architecting changes — significant changes in business strategy, technology landscape, or regulatory requirements that cannot be accommodated by incremental updates.

---

**Q:** What is the relationship between Phase H and Phase G?
**A:** Phase G governs initial implementation. Phase H manages ongoing changes after deployment. Phase H may feed back into Phase G (for additional implementation) or to Phase A (for a new cycle).

---

**Q:** What drives changes in Phase H?
**A:** Business drivers (new strategy, M&A, regulatory), technology drivers (new technology, end-of-life), lessons learned from implementation, performance monitoring, and external environment changes.

---

## 11. Requirements Management

**Q:** What is the purpose of the Requirements Management phase?
**A:** To manage architecture requirements throughout the entire ADM cycle — identifying, storing, prioritizing, and feeding requirements into the relevant phases.

---

**Q:** Is Requirements Management a sequential phase?
**A:** No. It operates continuously at the center of the ADM cycle, interacting with every phase throughout the process.

---

**Q:** What are the key activities of Requirements Management?
**A:** Identifying requirements, baselining requirements, monitoring baseline requirements, modifying requirements as needed, dispensing with requirements that are no longer relevant, and ensuring each ADM phase addresses the current requirements.

---

**Q:** What is the key output of Requirements Management?
**A:** The Architecture Requirements Specification — a document that captures all architecture requirements, updated as the ADM progresses through each phase.

---

**Q:** How does Requirements Management interact with the ADM phases?
**A:** Every phase generates, consumes, and may modify requirements. Requirements Management ensures that changes to requirements are properly assessed, tracked, and communicated to affected phases.

---

**Q:** What is the Architecture Requirements Specification?
**A:** A deliverable that provides a quantitative (measurable) set of requirements capturing the properties of the architecture. It includes functional, non-functional, and constraint requirements.

## 12. Tricky Exam-Style Questions

**Q:** In which phase is the Statement of Architecture Work created?
**A:** Phase A — Architecture Vision.

---

**Q:** In which phase is the Architecture Definition Document substantially completed?
**A:** Phase D — Technology Architecture (it accumulates content from Phases B, C, and D).

---

**Q:** In which phase are Transition Architectures first created?
**A:** Phase E — Opportunities and Solutions.

---

**Q:** In which phase is the Implementation and Migration Plan finalized?
**A:** Phase F — Migration Planning.

---

**Q:** Which phase creates Architecture Contracts?
**A:** Phase G — Implementation Governance.

---

**Q:** Which phase handles ongoing changes after deployment?
**A:** Phase H — Architecture Change Management.

---

**Q:** Which phase defines architecture principles?
**A:** The Preliminary Phase.

---

**Q:** Which phase validates that implementations conform to the architecture?
**A:** Phase G — Implementation Governance (through compliance reviews).

---

**Q:** In which phase is Gap Analysis first performed?
**A:** Phase B — Business Architecture (then repeated in Phases C and D).

---

**Q:** Which phase is responsible for identifying work packages and projects?
**A:** Phase E — Opportunities and Solutions.

---

**Q:** Which phase prioritizes projects based on business value and risk?
**A:** Phase F — Migration Planning.

---

**Q:** In which phases are Architecture Building Blocks (ABBs) defined?
**A:** Phases B, C, and D (the architecture definition phases).

---

**Q:** In which phase are Solution Building Blocks (SBBs) first identified?
**A:** Phase E — Opportunities and Solutions.

---

**Q:** Which phase produces the Architecture Vision document?
**A:** Phase A — Architecture Vision.

---

**Q:** What is the first phase to produce a draft Architecture Roadmap?
**A:** Components of the Architecture Roadmap emerge from Phases B, C, and D. The consolidated Architecture Roadmap is first produced in Phase E.

---

**Q:** Which phase uses Business Scenarios as a key technique?
**A:** Phase A — Architecture Vision.

---

**Q:** Which phase determines if a change requires a new ADM cycle?
**A:** Phase H — Architecture Change Management.

---

**Q:** If a compliance review in Phase G reveals a major deviation, what happens?
**A:** A dispensation may be granted, the project may be required to remediate, or a change request is raised. If the deviation is fundamental, it may trigger a return to an earlier phase or a new ADM cycle via Phase H.

---

**Q:** Can you skip phases in the ADM?
**A:** Yes. TOGAF is adaptable — phases can be tailored, abbreviated, or skipped based on organizational need. However, each skipped phase should have a documented rationale.

---

**Q:** What is the correct order for the "architecture definition" phases?
**A:** B (Business) → C (Information Systems: Data + Application) → D (Technology). This is the standard sequence, though TOGAF allows flexibility.

---

**Q:** What is the correct order for the "implementation planning" phases?
**A:** E (Opportunities & Solutions) → F (Migration Planning) → G (Implementation Governance) → H (Architecture Change Management).

## 13. Phase Relationships and Dependencies

**Q:** What does Phase A receive from the Preliminary Phase?
**A:** Architecture Principles, Tailored Architecture Framework, Organizational Model for EA, Architecture Repository (initial), and the Request for Architecture Work.

---

**Q:** What does Phase B receive from Phase A?
**A:** The Architecture Vision, Statement of Architecture Work, approved scope, stakeholder map, and high-level baseline and target business architecture.

---

**Q:** What does Phase C receive from Phase B?
**A:** The Business Architecture (baseline and target), Gap Analysis results, updated Architecture Requirements Specification, and business architecture components of the Architecture Roadmap.

---

**Q:** What does Phase D receive from Phase C?
**A:** The Data and Application Architectures (baseline and target), Gap Analysis results, updated Architecture Requirements Specification, and information systems components of the Architecture Roadmap.

---

**Q:** What does Phase E receive from Phase D?
**A:** The complete Architecture Definition Document (all four domains), all Gap Analysis results (B, C, D), the Architecture Requirements Specification, and Architecture Roadmap components.

---

**Q:** What does Phase F receive from Phase E?
**A:** The consolidated Architecture Roadmap, Transition Architectures, Implementation and Migration Strategy, and identified work packages/projects.

---

**Q:** What does Phase G receive from Phase F?
**A:** The finalized Implementation and Migration Plan, Architecture Contracts (draft), Architecture Definition Document, and Architecture Requirements Specification.

---

**Q:** What does Phase H receive from Phase G?
**A:** Architecture Compliance reviews, Architecture Contracts (signed), implemented solutions, and change requests.

---

**Q:** How does Phase H feed back to Phase A?
**A:** When Phase H determines a re-architecting change is needed, it generates a new Request for Architecture Work that triggers Phase A of a new ADM cycle.

---

**Q:** Which phases form the "Architecture Definition" iteration?
**A:** Phases B, C, and D — where the detailed architectures for all four domains are developed.

---

**Q:** Which phases form the "Transition Planning" iteration?
**A:** Phases E and F — where the migration path and implementation projects are planned.

---

**Q:** Which phases form the "Architecture Governance" iteration?
**A:** Phases G and H — where implementation oversight and change management are performed.

---

**Q:** What is the key feedback loop in the ADM?
**A:** Phase H can loop back to any phase — most commonly to Phase A (for a new cycle) or to Phases B–D (for incremental updates to architecture definitions).

---

**Q:** How does Requirements Management relate to all phases?
**A:** It operates at the center of the ADM, continuously interacting with every phase. Each phase may generate new requirements, consume existing ones, or trigger modifications to requirements.

---

**Q:** Exam trap: Is the Preliminary Phase considered "Phase 0"?
**A:** No. The Preliminary Phase is not numbered. It is a distinct preparatory phase that precedes the numbered phases (A–H). Do not confuse it with "Phase 0."

---

**Q:** Exam trap: Does Phase C produce one architecture or two?
**A:** Two: Data Architecture and Application Architecture. These are developed within Phase C but are considered two separate architecture domains under the umbrella of Information Systems Architectures.

---

**Q:** Exam trap: Which phase produces the Architecture Roadmap — E or F?
**A:** Both. Phase E produces the initial, consolidated Architecture Roadmap. Phase F finalizes and refines it. On the exam, if asked where the roadmap is "finalized," the answer is Phase F.

---

**Q:** Exam trap: Architecture Governance — is it a phase or a capability?
**A:** Both. Phase G is called "Implementation Governance" and focuses on governing implementation projects. "Architecture Governance" is also a broader capability described in the Architecture Capability Framework that spans the entire ADM.

---

**Q:** Exam trap: What is the difference between Phase G and Phase H?
**A:** Phase G governs the implementation of the architecture (ensuring projects build what was planned). Phase H manages changes to the architecture after implementation (ensuring the architecture evolves properly).
