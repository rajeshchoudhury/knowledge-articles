# TOGAF 10 Certification — 12-Week Study Plan

> **A structured, day-by-day plan to prepare for both Part 1 (Foundation) and Part 2 (Certified) exams.**

---

## How to Use This Plan

- **Daily commitment**: 1–2 hours on weekdays, 2–3 hours on weekends
- **Total study hours**: approximately 120–150 hours over 12 weeks
- **Structure**: Foundation concepts first (Weeks 1–4), ADM deep dive (Weeks 5–8), advanced topics and exam practice (Weeks 9–12)
- **Flashcards**: reviewed daily using spaced repetition (start in Week 1, continue through exam day)
- **Practice tests**: introduced in Week 5, intensifying through Week 12
- **Self-assessments**: checkpoint quizzes at Weeks 4, 8, and 11

### Adjusting for Your Experience Level

| Your Background | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| **Senior EA practitioner** | Skim Weeks 1–2, spend more time on practice tests; consider an 8-week plan |
| **IT professional, no EA experience** | Follow the plan as written; add an extra week if needed |
| **New to IT/Architecture** | Extend to 14–16 weeks; spend extra time on Weeks 1–4 |
| **Retaking the exam** | Focus on your weak areas; jump to practice tests sooner |

## Spaced Repetition Schedule for Flashcards

Flashcards are one of the most effective tools for memorizing TOGAF terminology and ADM phase details. Follow this spaced repetition protocol:

### How Spaced Repetition Works

When you first learn a card, review it the next day. If you get it right, increase the interval. If wrong, reset to one day.

```
Day 1:  Learn new cards
Day 2:  Review (if correct → next review in 3 days)
Day 5:  Review (if correct → next review in 7 days)
Day 12: Review (if correct → next review in 14 days)
Day 26: Review (if correct → next review in 30 days)
```

### Weekly Flashcard Schedule

| Week | New Cards to Learn | Review Load (approximate) | Decks |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 30 cards from Deck A | 30 reviews/day by end of week | A |
| 2 | 30 cards from Deck A + 20 from Deck B | 40 reviews/day | A, B |
| 3 | 20 cards from Deck B | 40 reviews/day | A, B |
| 4 | 20 cards from Deck B | 35 reviews/day (older cards graduating) | A, B |
| 5 | 20 cards from Deck C | 35 reviews/day | A, B, C |
| 6 | 20 cards from Deck C | 35 reviews/day | A, B, C |
| 7 | 20 cards from Deck F | 35 reviews/day | A, B, C, F |
| 8 | 20 cards from Deck C + F | 40 reviews/day | A, B, C, F |
| 9 | 20 cards from Deck D + 15 from Deck E | 40 reviews/day | All decks |
| 10 | 15 cards from Deck D + E | 35 reviews/day | All decks |
| 11 | No new cards — review only | 30 reviews/day | All decks |
| 12 | No new cards — focus on difficult cards | 20 reviews/day | All decks |

**Tip**: Use a spaced repetition app like Anki, Quizlet, or a physical flashcard box to manage intervals automatically.

## Tips by Learning Style

### Visual Learners

- **Draw the ADM cycle** at least once per week from memory
- **Create color-coded mind maps** for each ADM phase (inputs in green, steps in blue, outputs in red)
- **Study the reference diagrams** — Enterprise Continuum, TRM, III-RM, Content Metamodel
- **Use whiteboard sketches** to trace how artifacts flow between phases
- **Watch video tutorials** as a supplement to reading

### Reading/Writing Learners

- **Write one-page summaries** for each module after reading
- **Create comparison tables** — e.g., Phase A vs. Phase B, deliverables vs. artifacts
- **Rewrite key definitions** in your own words
- **Keep a study journal** tracking daily progress, questions, and insights
- **Write practice essay answers** explaining how the ADM applies to a scenario

### Auditory Learners

- **Read definitions aloud** — hearing them reinforces memory
- **Record voice memos** explaining ADM phases; listen during commutes
- **Join or form a study group** to discuss concepts
- **Explain TOGAF concepts to a non-technical friend** — if you can explain it simply, you understand it
- **Use podcasts** on enterprise architecture as supplementary material

### Kinesthetic/Practical Learners

- **Apply TOGAF to a real project** at work (even informally)
- **Walk through each ADM phase** using your own organization as an example
- **Create physical flashcards** you can shuffle and sort
- **Role-play stakeholder scenarios** — practice explaining architecture decisions
- **Build a mock Architecture Repository** using folders and documents

## Last-Minute Revision Strategy (Final 48 Hours)

### Two Days Before the Exam

1. **Quick skim of all one-page module summaries** (if you wrote them — highly recommended)
2. **Review your "weak areas" list** from Checkpoint #3 — focus only on these
3. **Run through 30 flashcards** — pick the ones you've gotten wrong most often
4. **Take one final timed practice test** — but don't stress about the score; this is about rhythm
5. **Total study time**: 2 hours maximum

### The Day Before the Exam

1. **Review the top 20 most important flashcards only**
2. **Skim the ADM cycle diagram** and ensure you can recreate it
3. **Review the exam logistics**: location/link, start time, ID requirements
4. **Stop studying by 6 PM** — your brain needs time to consolidate
5. **Prepare everything you need** for exam day (ID, water, confirmation email)
6. **Get at least 7–8 hours of sleep**
7. **Total study time**: 30–45 minutes maximum

### Exam Morning

1. **Light breakfast** — protein and complex carbs (avoid sugar crashes)
2. **No cramming** — reviewing new material will only create anxiety
3. **Arrive/log in 30 minutes early**
4. **Take three deep breaths** before starting
5. **Trust your preparation**

## What If I'm Behind Schedule?

- **1–2 days behind**: Skip weekend summaries; focus on modules and flashcards
- **1 week behind**: Combine two closely related modules (e.g., read Phases E and F together)
- **2+ weeks behind**: Consider pushing your exam date back; it's better to be well-prepared than to rush
- **Consistently scoring below 50%**: Return to the foundational modules; ensure you truly understand the ADM cycle before moving to advanced topics
- **Strong in Foundation but weak in Certified**: Spend extra time on scenario analysis technique; practice ranking answers rather than choosing a single "right" answer
